Section 01

Review: Building Confidence Intervals via the CLT

We return to the kiss-direction example: a sample \(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Bernoulli}(p)\), where \(p\) is unknown. Our estimator for \(p\) is the sample mean:

Estimator
\[\hat{p} = \bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i\]

Since \(\hat{p}\) is a random variable (an average of random variables), we want to characterize its distribution. The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) provides this characterization for large \(n\):

Central Limit Theorem — Bernoulli Case
\[\sqrt{n}\;\frac{\bar{X}_n - p}{\sqrt{p(1-p)}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty\]

In words: the standardized sample mean converges in distribution to a standard normal. Note the denominator is the standard deviation of \(\bar{X}_n\), since:

  • \(\operatorname{Var}(\bar{X}_n) = \frac{p(1-p)}{n}\), so \(\operatorname{SD}(\bar{X}_n) = \sqrt{p(1-p)/n}\).
  • Multiplying by \(\sqrt{n}\) removes the \(n\) in the denominator, leaving \(\sqrt{p(1-p)}\).

Constructing the Confidence Interval

From the CLT, for large \(n\):

Coverage Probability Statement
\[P\!\left(\left|\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - p)}{\sqrt{p(1-p)}}\right| \leq q_{\alpha/2}\right) \approx 1 - \alpha\]

where \(q_{\alpha/2}\) is the upper \(\alpha/2\) quantile of the standard normal: \(P(Z > q_{\alpha/2}) = \alpha/2\). Solving this inequality for \(p\) gives a confidence interval. Two practical approaches exist:

  1. Solve the quadratic in \(p\) — exact but algebraically involved (gives the Agresti–Coull-type interval).
  2. Bound \(p(1-p) \leq \tfrac{1}{4}\) — since \(p(1-p) \leq \tfrac{1}{4}\) for all \(p \in [0,1]\), giving the conservative interval: \[\hat{p} \;\pm\; \frac{q_{\alpha/2}}{2\sqrt{n}}\]
★ Key Insight — Why does standardization work?
If we failed to subtract the mean \(p\) or forgot to divide by \(\sqrt{p(1-p)}\), the limiting distribution would still depend on \(p\) — an unknown parameter. Standardization makes the limit parameter-free. This is the core idea of a pivotal quantity.
Section 02

Pivotal Distributions

D Definition — Pivotal Quantity
A statistic \(T(X_1,\ldots,X_n;\,\theta)\) is called a pivotal quantity (or pivot) if its distribution does not depend on any unknown parameters. A pivot may depend exactly (finite \(n\)) or asymptotically (large \(n\)) on no unknowns.

The standardized sample mean \(\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - p)/\sqrt{p(1-p)}\) is an asymptotic pivot — its limiting distribution \(\mathcal{N}(0,1)\) depends on nothing unknown. This is powerful: we can read quantiles directly from the standard normal table.

The beauty of the Gaussian distribution in this context is that it has been exhaustively tabulated. For arbitrary distributions, as long as the limiting distribution is parameter-free, we can always compute quantiles via simulation.

★ Key Insight
The goal of asymptotic statistics is: take a good estimator (like \(\bar{X}_n\)) and transform it into a quantity whose limiting distribution is known and parameter-free. That transformation is the pivot.
Section 03

Exact Distribution for Small \(n\) — A Counting Argument

When the sample size is small (e.g., \(n = 3\)), the CLT approximation is not valid. However, we can compute the exact distribution of \(\bar{X}_3\).

Enumerating Values of \(\bar{X}_3\)

With \(X_1, X_2, X_3 \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Bernoulli}(p)\), each \(X_i \in \{0,1\}\). Their sum \(S = X_1+X_2+X_3\) takes values \(\{0,1,2,3\}\), giving \(\bar{X}_3 = S/3 \in \{0, \tfrac{1}{3}, \tfrac{2}{3}, 1\}\). The number of ways to achieve each value follows from combinatorics:

\(\bar{X}_3\) # of 1's in sample Count (combinations) \(P(\bar{X}_3 = k)\)
001\((1-p)^3\)
\(\tfrac{1}{3}\)13\(\binom{3}{1}p(1-p)^2\)
\(\tfrac{2}{3}\)23\(\binom{3}{2}p^2(1-p)\)
131\(p^3\)

The counts \(1, 3, 3, 1\) are the binomial coefficients \(\binom{3}{k}\). In fact, \(3\bar{X}_3 \sim \text{Binomial}(3, p)\). The problem is that these probabilities depend on \(p\), so this distribution is not a pivot — finding confidence intervals requires either solving for \(p\) or using bounds (like Hoeffding's inequality, discussed next).

Section 04

Hoeffding's Inequality — A Non-Asymptotic Bound

T Theorem — Hoeffding's Inequality
Let \(X_1, \ldots, X_n\) be independent random variables with \(a_i \leq X_i \leq b_i\) almost surely. Then for any \(\varepsilon > 0\): \[P\!\left(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \varepsilon\right) \;\leq\; 2\exp\!\left(\!-\frac{2n^2\varepsilon^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n (b_i-a_i)^2}\right)\] For i.i.d. Bernoulli\((p)\) variables (\(a=0, b=1\)): \(\displaystyle P\!\left(|\bar{X}_n - p| \geq \varepsilon\right) \leq 2e^{-2n\varepsilon^2}.\)

This inequality is non-asymptotic: it holds for every fixed \(n\), not just in the limit. It provides a tail bound on \(\bar{X}_n\) without requiring normality.

★ Geometric Intuition
The density of \(\bar{X}_n\) (even if it lacks a smooth density) is always bounded above by a Gaussian-shaped envelope. Hoeffding quantifies this: the tails of any bounded-variable average decay at least as fast as Gaussian tails. The bound is slightly looser than the CLT-based interval by a factor related to \(\sqrt{2\log(2/\alpha)}\) vs \(q_{\alpha/2}\).

Deriving a Confidence Interval via Hoeffding

Set the bound equal to \(\alpha\) and solve for \(\varepsilon\):

1
Set \(2e^{-2n\varepsilon^2} = \alpha\)
We want the tail probability to equal our significance level.
2
\(e^{-2n\varepsilon^2} = \alpha/2\), so \(-2n\varepsilon^2 = \log(\alpha/2)\)
3
\(\varepsilon = \sqrt{\dfrac{\log(2/\alpha)}{2n}}\)

This gives the Hoeffding confidence interval:

Hoeffding Confidence Interval (level ≥ 1−α)
\[P\!\left(p \in \left[\bar{X}_n - \sqrt{\frac{\log(2/\alpha)}{2n}},\;\bar{X}_n + \sqrt{\frac{\log(2/\alpha)}{2n}}\right]\right) \geq 1 - \alpha\]

Comparing the two margin-of-error formulas:

MethodMargin of ErrorValiditySharpness
CLT (conservative bound) \(\dfrac{q_{\alpha/2}}{2\sqrt{n}}\) Asymptotic (\(n \geq 30\)) Sharper
Hoeffding \(\sqrt{\dfrac{\log(2/\alpha)}{2n}}\) Any \(n\) Conservative (larger)
! Important Trade-off
The CLT-based interval is narrower (sharper), but relies on the approximation being valid. Hoeffding's interval is always valid — but pays a price in width. In statistics, stronger assumptions enable tighter conclusions. Hoeffding's approach avoids distributional assumptions entirely.
Section 05

Types of Convergence for Random Variables

Why do we need different convergence modes? Because random variables live on a probability space, and there are different ways to ask "does \(T_n\) get close to \(T\)?" — depending on how strictly you handle the randomness.

D Probability Space Intuition
Think of \(\omega\) (omega) as the underlying random object — like a ball of snow. A random variable is a measurable function of \(\omega\): the volume, temperature, or surface area of that ball. Different realizations of the experiment give different \(\omega\)'s.

1. Almost Sure Convergence (a.s.)

Definition
\[T_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} T \iff P\!\left(\omega : T_n(\omega) \to T(\omega)\right) = 1\]

Meaning: For (almost) every possible realization of the underlying randomness, the sequence \(T_n\) converges to \(T\). The set of "bad" outcomes where it fails has probability zero. This is the strongest mode — it says convergence happens everywhere randomness permits.

2. Convergence in Probability

Definition
\[T_n \xrightarrow{P} T \iff \forall\,\varepsilon > 0,\quad P(|T_n - T| > \varepsilon) \to 0 \text{ as } n \to \infty\]

Meaning: For each fixed \(n\), there is a small probability that \(T_n\) deviates from \(T\), but this probability shrinks to zero. Slightly weaker than a.s. convergence because it tolerates a residual probability at each \(n\) (as long as it vanishes).

3. Convergence in \(L^p\)

Definition
\[T_n \xrightarrow{L^p} T \iff \mathbb{E}\!\left[|T_n - T|^p\right] \to 0 \text{ as } n \to \infty\]

Meaning: The \(p\)-th moment of the distance goes to zero. The most common case is \(L^2\): if \(\mathbb{E}[T_n] = 0\) for all \(n\) and \(\operatorname{Var}(T_n) \to 0\), then \(T_n \xrightarrow{L^2} 0\). Intuitively, the random variable concentrates around its mean as its spread vanishes.

Key property: \(L^p\) convergence implies \(L^q\) convergence for all \(q \leq p\).

4. Convergence in Distribution (the one we use most)

Definition
\[T_n \xrightarrow{d} T \iff \forall x \text{ where } F_T \text{ is continuous:}\quad F_{T_n}(x) \to F_T(x)\]

Meaning: The cumulative distribution functions converge pointwise at all continuity points. This says nothing about the sample paths \(T_n(\omega)\) themselves — only that the laws (probability distributions) converge.

E Example — Why Convergence in Distribution is the Weakest
Let \(X \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)\). Then \(X \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)\) and \(-X \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)\) (by symmetry). Yet \(X + (-X) = 0\) almost surely — certainly not distributed as \(\mathcal{N}(0,2)\). This shows you cannot add limits under mere convergence in distribution. The realizations of \(X\) and \(-X\) are perfectly anticorrelated, but their marginal distributions are identical.

Equivalent Characterizations of Convergence in Distribution

The following are equivalent to \(T_n \xrightarrow{d} T\):

  1. CDF convergence: \(F_{T_n}(x) \to F_T(x)\) at all continuity points (definition above).
  2. Bounded continuous functions: For every bounded continuous \(f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\), \[\mathbb{E}[f(T_n)] \to \mathbb{E}[f(T)].\]
  3. Characteristic functions: For all \(x \in \mathbb{R}\), \[\mathbb{E}[e^{ixT_n}] \to \mathbb{E}[e^{ixT}].\] The CLT is most elegantly proved using this: expand the characteristic function of \(\bar{X}_n\) in a Taylor series and recognize \(e^{-x^2/2}\) — the characteristic function of \(\mathcal{N}(0,1)\) — as the second-order term.
★ Why Convergence in Distribution Suffices for Statistics
In statistics, we only need to compute probabilities of events involving our estimators. Convergence in distribution guarantees that these probabilities match those of the limiting distribution. We do not need to know that every realization converges — only that the statistical behavior converges.

The different modes of convergence are related by implication. Stronger modes imply weaker ones:

⟶

⟶

Every arrow represents: "if converges on the left, also converges on the right"

Key caveat: For almost sure, \(L^p\), and in-probability convergence, the limits are "equal almost surely" — the same random variable. For convergence in distribution, the limit is only identified up to its law, not as a specific random variable.

Continuous Mapping Theorem (CMT)

T Theorem — Continuous Mapping
If \(T_n \to T\) (in any of the above senses except \(L^p\)) and \(f\) is a continuous function, then \(f(T_n) \to f(T)\) in the same sense.

This is extremely useful: if \(\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} \mu\), then \(1/\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} 1/\mu\) (since \(x \mapsto 1/x\) is continuous away from 0). Similarly for convergence in probability.

! Why This Matters
The CLT gives us convergence in distribution. But we often need to combine our CLT result with another quantity (e.g., replacing \(p\) with \(\hat{p}\)). Since convergence in distribution does not generally support arithmetic operations between sequences, we need Slutsky's Theorem.
Section 08

Slutsky's Theorem

T Theorem — Slutsky
Let \(X_n \xrightarrow{d} X\) and \(Y_n \xrightarrow{P} c\) where \(c\) is a deterministic constant (not a random variable). Then: \[X_n + Y_n \xrightarrow{d} X + c \qquad \text{and} \qquad X_n Y_n \xrightarrow{d} cX\] More generally, the vector \((X_n, Y_n) \xrightarrow{d} (X, c)\), so any continuous function of the pair converges in distribution to that function applied to the limit.
★ Why the Constant Matters
Slutsky's theorem works precisely because \(Y_n\) converges to a constant \(c\) — a degenerate random variable. A constant converges both in probability and in distribution (trivially), and because it is non-random, the joint convergence \((X_n, Y_n) \xrightarrow{d} (X, c)\) follows. This would fail if \(Y_n\) converged only in distribution to a non-degenerate random variable.

The Primary Use Case: Plug-In Arguments

Consider the standardized estimator:

CLT Statement
\[\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - p)}{\sqrt{p(1-p)}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)\]

We cannot compute this pivot because \(p\) is unknown. We want to replace \(p\) with \(\hat{p} = \bar{X}_n\). Write:

Decomposition
\[\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - p)}{\sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}} = \underbrace{\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - p)}{\sqrt{p(1-p)}}}_{\xrightarrow{d}\,\mathcal{N}(0,1)} \times \underbrace{\frac{\sqrt{p(1-p)}}{\sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}}}_{\xrightarrow{P}\,1 \text{ (LLN + CMT)}}\]

By Slutsky's theorem (the second factor converges in probability to the constant 1), the product converges in distribution to \(\mathcal{N}(0,1) \times 1 = \mathcal{N}(0,1)\). Hence the plug-in pivot is still asymptotically standard normal.

! How to Use "By Slutsky" in Your Work
Write "by Slutsky" only when you are genuinely combining a sequence that converges in distribution with another that converges in probability to a constant. Do not invoke it for purely deterministic or algebraic steps.
Section 09

Case Study: Exponential Model — Confidence Interval for \(\lambda\)

Setup

Observe inter-arrival times \(T_1, \ldots, T_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Exponential}(\lambda)\). The exponential density is:

Exponential Density
\[f(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t}, \quad t > 0, \quad \lambda > 0\]

Key properties:

  • \(\mathbb{E}[T_i] = 1/\lambda\), \(\quad\operatorname{Var}(T_i) = 1/\lambda^2\).
  • Memoryless property: \(P(T_1 > t + s \mid T_1 > t) = P(T_1 > s)\). Given you have already waited \(t\) minutes, the remaining wait time has the same distribution as the original wait. This is unique to the exponential among continuous distributions.
  • The exponential distribution is widely used for inter-arrival times in queuing theory and call centers.

Estimating \(\lambda\)

Since \(\mathbb{E}[T_i] = 1/\lambda\), we estimate \(1/\lambda\) by \(\bar{T}_n\), and therefore estimate \(\lambda\) by:

Estimator for λ
\[\hat{\lambda} = \frac{1}{\bar{T}_n}\]

This follows the universal heuristic: replace expectations by sample averages, then invert if needed.

Asymptotic Distribution of \(\hat{\lambda}\)

By the CLT applied to \(\bar{T}_n\) (with mean \(1/\lambda\) and variance \(1/\lambda^2\)):

CLT for the Sample Mean
\[\sqrt{n}\,\frac{\bar{T}_n - 1/\lambda}{1/\lambda} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)\]

Rearranging (multiply by \(\lambda\)):

Equivalent CLT Statement
\[\sqrt{n}\,\lambda(\bar{T}_n - 1/\lambda) = \sqrt{n}\,(\lambda\bar{T}_n - 1) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)\]

Direct Confidence Interval for \(\lambda\)

From the above pivot, with probability \(1-\alpha\):

\[|\sqrt{n}\,\lambda\bar{T}_n - \sqrt{n}| \leq q_{\alpha/2}\]

Solving for \(\lambda\):

CI for λ (direct inversion)
\[\lambda \in \left[\frac{1 - q_{\alpha/2}/\sqrt{n}}{\bar{T}_n},\;\frac{1 + q_{\alpha/2}/\sqrt{n}}{\bar{T}_n}\right] = \frac{1}{\bar{T}_n}\left(1 \pm \frac{q_{\alpha/2}}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\]

Note: the width of the interval depends on \(\bar{T}_n\) itself — this is natural, since \(\bar{T}_n\) carries information about both the mean and variance of the exponential distribution.

Section 10

The Delta Method

The Delta Method lets us derive the asymptotic distribution of a function of an asymptotically normal estimator. It is essentially a first-order Taylor expansion argument.

T Theorem — Delta Method
Suppose \(\sqrt{n}(Z_n - \theta) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)\). Let \(g\) be a differentiable function with \(g'(\theta) \neq 0\) and \(g'\) continuous at \(\theta\). Then: \[\sqrt{n}\bigl(g(Z_n) - g(\theta)\bigr) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\;\sigma^2 [g'(\theta)]^2\right)\]

Proof Sketch

1
First-order Taylor expansion: By the Mean Value Theorem, there exists \(\bar{\theta}\) between \(Z_n\) and \(\theta\) such that: \[g(Z_n) = g(\theta) + (Z_n - \theta)\,g'(\bar{\theta})\]
This is exact, not approximate — the MVT guarantees such a \(\bar{\theta}\) exists.
2
Multiply by \(\sqrt{n}\): \[\sqrt{n}\bigl(g(Z_n) - g(\theta)\bigr) = \underbrace{\sqrt{n}(Z_n - \theta)}_{\xrightarrow{d}\;\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)} \times \underbrace{g'(\bar{\theta})}_{\xrightarrow{P}\;g'(\theta)}\]
3
Convergence of \(\bar\theta\): Since \(Z_n \xrightarrow{P} \theta\) (by LLN) and \(\bar{\theta}\) is sandwiched between \(Z_n\) and \(\theta\), we have \(\bar{\theta} \xrightarrow{P} \theta\) (squeeze theorem). By continuity of \(g'\) and CMT: \(g'(\bar{\theta}) \xrightarrow{P} g'(\theta)\).
This is where continuity of g' is essential.
4
Apply Slutsky: We have (convergence in distribution) × (convergence in probability to a constant). By Slutsky's theorem: \[\sqrt{n}\bigl(g(Z_n)-g(\theta)\bigr) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2) \times g'(\theta) = \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\sigma^2[g'(\theta)]^2\right)\]

Application: Asymptotic Distribution of \(\hat{\lambda}\)

Apply the Delta Method with \(g(x) = 1/x\), \(Z_n = \bar{T}_n\), \(\theta = 1/\lambda\), and \(\sigma^2 = 1/\lambda^2\).

  • \(g'(x) = -1/x^2\)
  • \(g'(\theta) = g'(1/\lambda) = -\lambda^2\)
  • \([g'(\theta)]^2 = \lambda^4\)
  • Asymptotic variance: \(\sigma^2 \cdot [g'(\theta)]^2 = \frac{1}{\lambda^2} \cdot \lambda^4 = \lambda^2\)
Asymptotic Normality of λ̂
\[\sqrt{n}\,(\hat{\lambda} - \lambda) = \sqrt{n}\!\left(\frac{1}{\bar{T}_n} - \lambda\right) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,\,\lambda^2)\]

Plug-In Confidence Interval via Slutsky

The pivot \(\sqrt{n}(\hat\lambda - \lambda)/\lambda\) converges to \(\mathcal{N}(0,1)\). Since \(\lambda\) is unknown, we replace it with \(\hat{\lambda}\). Write:

Decomposition (for Slutsky)
\[\frac{\sqrt{n}(\hat\lambda - \lambda)}{\hat\lambda} = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\hat\lambda - \lambda)}{\lambda} \times \frac{\lambda}{\hat\lambda} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1) \times 1 = \mathcal{N}(0,1)\]

Justification: \(\lambda/\hat\lambda \xrightarrow{P} 1\) by LLN + CMT, and \(\lambda/\hat\lambda\) converges to the constant 1. By Slutsky, the product converges to \(\mathcal{N}(0,1)\).

This yields the plug-in confidence interval:

Asymptotic CI for λ (plug-in)
\[\hat\lambda \;\pm\; \frac{q_{\alpha/2}\,\hat\lambda}{\sqrt{n}} = \hat\lambda\left(1 \pm \frac{q_{\alpha/2}}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\]

This is exactly the same as the interval obtained by direct inversion earlier, confirming consistency of the methods.

★ Summary — When to Use Each Tool
  • CLT alone: asymptotic normality of \(\bar{X}_n\). Pivot requires knowing the variance.
  • Delta Method: extends CLT to functions of \(\bar{X}_n\). Cost: multiply variance by \([g'(\theta)]^2\).
  • Slutsky: replaces unknown parameters in the pivot by consistent estimators. Justification: the ratio converges in probability to the constant 1.
  • CMT: if \(Z_n \to Z\), then \(f(Z_n) \to f(Z)\) for continuous \(f\). Used to show \(\hat\lambda \to \lambda\).
  • Hoeffding: no asymptotic argument needed; works for any \(n\) but gives a wider interval.
