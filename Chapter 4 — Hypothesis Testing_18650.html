§ 01

Why Hypothesis Testing? Estimation vs. Testing

Up to this point the course focused on estimation: deriving the best numerical guess for an unknown parameter $\theta$. Maximum likelihood estimation was the central tool. In many cases — particularly for Gaussian models — the MLE turned out simply to be the sample average. Even when we already knew the answer intuitively, going through MLE confirmed the intuition and gave us tools to analyse the estimator's quality using the Central Limit Theorem.

Now we introduce a fundamentally different kind of question. Instead of asking "what is $\mu$?" we ask a yes/no question: "Is $\mu$ equal to some reference value, or has it changed?" This is parametric hypothesis testing — "parametric" because the claim is about a parameter of a known distributional family.

Key Shift in Perspective
In estimation, randomness (via the CLT) appeared only in the analysis of the estimator after it was designed. In testing, randomness enters both the design and the analysis. We must account for random fluctuations in order to build the test itself — not just to understand it afterwards. The CLT is no longer just a quality certificate; it is part of the test construction.

Hypothesis testing is the foundation of essentially all data-driven scientific inquiry. Any time you formulate a scientific claim and try to confirm or deny it with data, you are implicitly doing a hypothesis test:

  • "Knocking down this gene cures melanoma." → collect patient outcomes → yes/no answer.
  • "This drug reduces coughing." → clinical trial → yes/no answer.
  • "Runners were faster this year." → sample race times → yes/no answer.

Everyone has heard of hypothesis testing; here we make it precise. The three goals of statistical inference are: estimation (produces an approximate value), confidence intervals (find a range), and hypothesis tests (answer a yes/no question — for example, "is it true that $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ are significantly different?"). This chapter focuses on the last.

§ 02

Motivating Example — The Cherry Blossom Run

The Credit Union Cherry Blossom Run is a 10-mile race held annually in Washington D.C. The 2009 and 2012 datasets are both publicly available in R.

Known Data from 2009 (Full Population, N ≈ 15,000)
  • Mean running time: $\mu_{2009} = 103.5$ minutes
  • Variance: $\sigma^2_{2009} = 373$
  • Distribution: histogram of all 15,000 times closely matches a Gaussian bell curve

The Problem

Suppose our budget allows surveying only $n = 10$ runners from the 2012 race. We want to know: were runners faster in 2012 than in 2009? How can we make a valid statistical claim from just 10 observations?

Where Randomness Comes From

We select $n$ runners uniformly at random. Each runner is an elementary outcome $\omega$ in the probability space — the runners are the omegas. The random variable of interest is their running time. This is how we inject randomness: not from the measurement itself, but from the random selection of who we observe.

From a given runner, many variables could be measured: country of origin, age, height. Here we care only about running time: $X_i = \text{running time of runner } i$.

Why the Gaussian Assumption Is Justified

The 2009 histogram is well-approximated by a Gaussian. This is expected: running time is the cumulative result of many small independent factors (physiological, environmental, psychological) — exactly the CLT setting. Bell-shaped distributions arise everywhere in nature for this reason. We therefore assume $X_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ for the 2012 data, with $\sigma^2 = 373$ fixed (same as 2009) and $\mu$ unknown.

Caveat on the Variance Assumption
Fixing $\sigma^2_{2012} = 373$ is acknowledged to be incorrect — the actual 2012 data has a different variance. This assumption is made for simplicity of exposition. The Gaussian shape assumption, by contrast, is quite robust: the CLT guarantees it arises whenever you aggregate many small independent effects, and this is hard to violate across years for the same race.

The Statistical Question

Cherry Blossom Hypothesis
$$H_0: \mu = 103.5 \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1: \mu < 103.5$$

We use a one-sided alternative because we ask specifically whether runners were faster (smaller time), not just different. This concentration of the alternative on one side increases statistical power.

§ 03

Two-Sample Test — Clinical Drug Trial

This is the more realistic scenario: no fixed reference value exists. Pharmaceutical companies face this problem daily. The FDA requires rigorously controlled trials before approving any drug.

Setup

A new cough syrup is tested against a placebo. Patients are randomly split:

  • Control group ($n_{\text{ctrl}}$ patients): receives maple syrup (placebo). Expected coughs/hour: $\mu_{\text{ctrl}}$.
  • Drug group ($n_{\text{drug}}$ patients): receives the real drug. Expected coughs/hour: $\mu_{\text{drug}}$.

Note that maple syrup itself has a soothing effect on coughing — it is sweet and helps slightly. The placebo effect is real but unknown. We cannot simply compare against zero; we must measure the placebo effect too.

Modeling Assumption
Cough counts are modeled as Poisson($\mu$): non-negative integers, fully characterised by their mean. This is natural for count data. So there is one unknown parameter per group.
Drug Trial Hypotheses
$$H_0: \mu_{\text{drug}} = \mu_{\text{ctrl}} \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1: \mu_{\text{drug}} < \mu_{\text{ctrl}}$$

Two Sources of Randomness

Unlike the Cherry Blossom case (one unknown vs. a known reference), here both means are estimated:

  • $\bar{X}^{\text{drug}}$ estimates $\mu_{\text{drug}}$ with fluctuation scale $1/\sqrt{n_{\text{drug}}}$
  • $\bar{X}^{\text{ctrl}}$ estimates $\mu_{\text{ctrl}}$ with fluctuation scale $1/\sqrt{n_{\text{ctrl}}}$

Any buffer in the test must account for both sources. The difference $\bar{X}^{\text{drug}} - \bar{X}^{\text{ctrl}}$ is approximately $\mathcal{N}(\mu_{\text{drug}} - \mu_{\text{ctrl}}, \sigma^2_{\text{combined}})$ and under $H_0$ is centred at zero.

Why We Make It Hard to Conclude the Drug Works

If $\mu_{\text{drug}} = \mu_{\text{ctrl}}$ (the drug is just corn syrup), the difference $\bar{X}^{\text{drug}} - \bar{X}^{\text{ctrl}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$. Without any buffer, this quantity is negative 50% of the time, meaning a naive test would approve 50% of all completely ineffective drugs. We demand the difference be substantially more negative than random chance could explain — far into the left tail of the null distribution.

Unequal Group Sizes

The two groups need not be the same size. This arises because:

  • Patient eligibility and dropouts are unpredictable
  • Ethical pressure: desperate patients prefer the real drug over placebo
  • Variance optimisation: if one group has higher variance, you want more subjects there

This is studied formally in the field of Design of Experiments.

§ 04

The Role of Modeling Assumptions

Modeling assumptions are not just conveniences — they are essential for hypothesis testing to work.

Why Restricting the Distribution Space Is Necessary

Consider the universe of all possible probability distributions. Without constraints, data will always find some distribution that fits better than any specific null. For example:

Why Overfitting Destroys Testing
Suppose you collect 10 running times: $\{91, 95, 102, \ldots\}$. The most likely distribution over all possible distributions is the discrete one placing mass $1/10$ on each observed value. This fits perfectly. You would always reject any Gaussian null — not because the data is non-Gaussian, but because you are searching across an enormous space. Any finite sample fits some exotic distribution perfectly by chance.

How Assumptions Sequentially Reduce the Problem

For the Cherry Blossom test, each assumption removes a dimension of uncertainty:

  1. Assume Gaussian: eliminates all non-Gaussian families (exponential, Poisson, discrete, etc.).
  2. Fix $\sigma^2 = 373$: reduces to a one-parameter family indexed by $\mu$ alone.
  3. One-sided alternative $\mu < 103.5$: removes the possibility $\mu > 103.5$ from consideration, focusing all power on detecting improvements.
The Fundamental Trade-off in Assumptions
More assumptions → more focused, more powerful test, but conclusions are more sensitive to those assumptions being correct. Fewer assumptions → more robust, but lower power. Good statistical practice means making the minimum set of defensible assumptions required to answer the specific question.
§ 05

Formal Hypothesis Formulation

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} P_\theta$ where $\theta \in \Theta$. Let $\Theta_0$ and $\Theta_1$ be two disjoint subsets of $\Theta$. Consider the two hypotheses:

Hypothesis Testing Problem
$$H_0 : \theta \in \Theta_0 \quad \text{(null hypothesis)}$$ $$H_1 : \theta \in \Theta_1 \quad \text{(alternative hypothesis)}$$ $$\Theta_0 \cap \Theta_1 = \emptyset, \quad \Theta_0, \Theta_1 \subseteq \Theta$$

We will always either be able to invalidate $H_0$ (reject it), or conclude that we cannot invalidate it. We never "accept $H_0$" — the only options are to reject or fail to reject. In this class, we do not care about $H_1$ directly: we focus entirely on controlling what happens under $H_0$.

Note that $\Theta_0 \cup \Theta_1$ need not equal all of $\Theta$ — there can be parameter values neither hypothesis covers (e.g., $\mu > 103.5$ in the Cherry Blossom example).

Example 16.1 — Fair Coin
Suppose $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Bernoulli}(p)$. We would like to test if $p = 1/2$. The hypotheses are: $$H_0: p = 1/2 \qquad H_1: p \ne 1/2$$ and the parameter sets are: $$\Theta_0 = \{1/2\} \qquad \Theta_1 = (0,1) \setminus \{1/2\}$$
Example — One-Sided Coin (Adversarial Setting)
Now suppose we want to know if the coin leans either way, and the professor wins if the coin is $p \ge 1/2$. The hypotheses are: $$H_0: p \ge 1/2 \qquad H_1: p < 1/2$$ and the parameter sets are $\Theta_0 = [1/2, 1)$ and $\Theta_1 = (0, 1/2)$.
Example — Smart Opponent
Suppose our professor is a smart opponent who would never play with a coin for which $p < 1/2$. More reasonable hypotheses are: $$H_0: p = 1/2 \qquad H_1: p < 1/2$$ with $\Theta_0 = \{1/2\}$ and $\Theta_1 = (0, 1/2)$. These do not cover the whole parameter space (since $p > 1/2$ is excluded) — but that is fine.
Example 16.2 — HIV Testing

People with HIV have extra antibodies, so we test by checking antibody concentration. We run many diluted samples, where each $X_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, and check if $\mu > \mu_0$ (our threshold). Suppose $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ where $\mu$ represents antibody concentration. The hypotheses are:

$$H_0: \mu \ge \mu_0 \qquad H_1: \mu < \mu_0$$ and the parameter sets are: $$\Theta_0 = [\mu_0, \infty) \times (0, \infty) \qquad \Theta_1 = (-\infty, \mu_0) \times (0, \infty)$$

Don't forget to include $\sigma^2$ in the parameter space!

There are two types of errors. A false negative means the patient is sick and we send them home — this is VERY dangerous. A false positive means the patient is fine and we treat them — not great, but not fatal. The null hypothesis should therefore be "the patient is sick," since we do not want to mistakenly reject this.

Asymmetry Between $H_0$ and $H_1$

  • $H_0$ is the conservative default position. We require evidence to overcome it.
  • If data is inconclusive, we side with $H_0$ — not because it is true, but because we lack evidence against it.
  • We never "accept $H_0$." We can only fail to reject it.
  • $H_1$ is the scientific claim — a discovery, an improvement, a new effect.
  • There is inherent asymmetry: in the case that $H_0$ is true, it is very dangerous or costly to reject it. This determines the assignment of $H_0$ and $H_1$.
The Use of H₁
Question: What is the use of defining $H_1$ if we know nothing about it? Short answer: no use in this class. Long answer: it would still be ideal to control false positives (patient is healthy and we treat them) since there may be nontrivial costs. However, false negatives are much more dangerous, so we focus on them in this class. In advanced settings, $H_1$ matters for computing power.
The "Innocent Until Proven Guilty" Analogy
H₀ = innocent. The prosecution must bring enough evidence to convince the jury. Without sufficient evidence, the verdict is "not guilty" — which is not the same as "innocent." O.J. Simpson was found not guilty, not proven innocent. The jury did not establish that he was innocent; they found the prosecution's evidence insufficient. This is exactly "failing to reject H₀."

§ 06

The Space of Distributions — A Geometric Picture

Think of all possible probability distributions as points in a vast abstract space. Every test partitions this space into $\Theta_0$ and $\Theta_1$.

Geometric Picture for the Cherry Blossom Example
  • Full space: contains every conceivable distribution — Exponential(0.1), Poisson(5), Bernoulli(0.3), arbitrary discrete distributions, etc.
  • After assuming Gaussian: reduces to the 2-parameter family $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$.
  • After fixing $\sigma^2 = 373$: reduces to a 1-parameter family indexed by $\mu$ alone — a line.
  • $\Theta_0 = \{\mu = 103.5\}$: a single point on this line.
  • $\Theta_1 = \{\mu < 103.5\}$: a half-line to the left.
  • $\{\mu > 103.5\}$: excluded — if the data points here, we simply fail to reject and make no statement about this region.
Why Restricting the Alternative Prevents False Discoveries
If you allowed the alternative to be any distribution in the entire space, then with $n=10$ data points, the most likely distribution is always the one that assigns probability $1/10$ to each observed value — you would always reject the Gaussian null. The point: the more alternatives you allow, the easier it is to find one that fits better than H₀ purely by chance. Restricting to a specific one-dimensional family (Gaussian with fixed variance) means the data can only speak to the one question you care about: has the mean changed?
§ 07

What Is a Test? Tests, Statistics, and Rejection Regions

The Test Function

A test is a statistic $\delta \in \{0, 1\}$ — a measurable function of the data that does not depend on any unknown parameter — such that:

Definition 16.4 — Test
$$\delta = 0 \Rightarrow H_0 \text{ is not rejected} \qquad \delta = 1 \Rightarrow H_0 \text{ is rejected}$$

As a memorisation tool (not entirely proper mathematics): $H_\delta$ is the hypothesis we keep. We still cannot conclude that it is true.

What Is a Statistic?

A statistic is a measurable function of the data alone. The practical test: if someone gives you numbers $x_1, \ldots, x_n$, can you compute it without knowing any unobserved quantities like the true $\mu$ or $p$? If yes, it is a statistic. This is why tests must be computable from data.

Tests should not depend on the true parameter $\theta$. For example, when we have a hypothesis like $H_0: \theta = \theta_0$, we can include $\theta_0$ because it is given, but we cannot use $\theta$, which is unknown. Generally, we can only achieve asymptotic levels $\alpha$, since we often use the CLT. In practice, this is a limitation because we may only have a few data points (say $n = 10$), which is not enough to apply results for $n \to \infty$. Also, rejecting $H_0$ does not imply accepting $H_1$ — for example, if our hypotheses are $p = 1/2$ and $p > 1/2$, they do not cover the whole space, and $p \ne 1/2 \not\Rightarrow p > 1/2$.

The Rejection Region

Definition 16.5 — Rejection Region
$$R_\delta = \{x \in \mathcal{E}^n : \delta(x) = 1\}$$ where $x = (x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ and $\mathcal{E}$ is the sample space.

The rejection region is the set of all possible outcomes that leads to rejection. For example, in the Bernoulli case with the test below, the rejection region is the set of all sequences $(x_1, \ldots, x_n) \in \{0,1\}^n$ such that the standardised statistic exceeds the threshold.

Constructing a Test for the Fair Coin

Recall the fair coin example: $H_0: p = 1/2$, $H_1: p \ne 1/2$. By the CLT combined with Slutsky's theorem:

CLT + Slutsky for Bernoulli
$$\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - p)}{\sqrt{\bar{X}_n(1 - \bar{X}_n)}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$$

If $H_0$ is true, we can say this quantity will likely fall between $(-2, 2)$ since the 0.975 quantile is 1.96. The test is:

Test δ (Slutsky denominator)
$$\delta = \mathbf{1}\!\left[\left|\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{x}_n - 0.5)}{\sqrt{\bar{x}_n(1-\bar{x}_n)}}\right| > c\right]$$

If we set $c = 1.96$, we reject $H_0$ when this statistic is unusually large — unlikely to come from the standard normal. Alternatively, since under $H_0: p = 1/2$ we can substitute the null value directly in the denominator:

Alternative Test δ' (null value in denominator)
$$\delta' = \mathbf{1}\!\left[2\sqrt{n}\,|\bar{x}_n - 0.5| > 1.96\right]$$

Note that $\delta$ is a function of $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ that produces a binary statistic.

Randomised Tests

There exists a concept of randomised tests that flip a coin at the boundary when the test statistic equals exactly $c$. For continuous data this situation has probability zero and is irrelevant. For discrete data (e.g., Bernoulli or Poisson), ties happen with positive probability. In practice, statisticians side with $H_0$ at the boundary — the most conservative choice.

Definition 16.6 — Type I Error
$$\alpha_\delta : \Theta_0 \to [0,1], \quad \alpha_\delta(\theta) = P_\theta(\delta = 1)$$
Definition 16.7 — Type II Error
$$\beta_\delta : \Theta_1 \to [0,1], \quad \beta_\delta(\theta) = P_\theta(\delta = 0)$$

Colloquially, Type I errors are false positives and Type II errors are false negatives. Both are functions of $\theta$, not single numbers.

Example 16.8 — Gaussian Mean Test

Suppose $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu, 1)$, where $\mu$ is unknown. Test $H_0: \mu = 0$ vs. $H_1: \mu \ne 0$.

Since the $X_i$ are Gaussian, $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ exactly. If $H_0$ were true, $\sqrt{n}\,\bar{X}_n \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. Let:

$$\delta = \mathbf{1}[|\sqrt{n}\,\bar{X}_n| > t]$$

The Type I error is:

$$\alpha_\delta(0) = P_0\bigl(|\sqrt{n}\,\bar{X}_n| > t\bigr) = 2 - 2\Phi(t)$$

We choose $t = q_{1-\alpha/2}$, the $(1-\alpha/2)$-quantile of $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$, so that $\alpha_\delta(0) = \alpha$.

Example — One-Sided Gaussian Test ($H_0: \mu \ge 0$)

Suppose instead we test $H_0: \mu \ge 0$ vs. $H_1: \mu < 0$. It is still true that $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. If $H_0$ is true (i.e., $\mu \ge 0$), then $\sqrt{n}\,\bar{X}_n \ge \sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)$, so we reject when $\sqrt{n}\,\bar{X}_n$ is too small:

$$\delta = \mathbf{1}[\sqrt{n}\,\bar{X}_n < -t]$$

For all $\mu \ge 0$:

$$\alpha_\delta(\mu) = P_\mu(\sqrt{n}\,\bar{X}_n < -t) \le P_\mu(\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) < -t) = \Phi(-t) = 1 - \Phi(t)$$

We take $t = q_{1-\alpha}$ so that $\alpha_\delta(\mu) \le \alpha$ for all $\mu \ge 0$. Equality is achieved when $\mu = 0$.

The Fundamental Trade-off

  • Test that never rejects ($\delta \equiv 0$): Type I error = 0%. Type II error = 100%. Every biased coin passes as fair. Useless.
  • Test that always rejects ($\delta \equiv 1$): Type I error = 100%. Type II error = 0%. Every fair coin is called biased. Also useless.
  • Both extremes are worthless. A useful test lies between them.
Why We Control Type I Error, Not Type II
We care most about the Type I error, so we define the level of a test via the Type I error. However, we would also like $(1 - \text{Type II error})$ to be as large as possible. After today we will never see the Type II error again in this class — this is "just for our culture."
§ 09

Power, Level, and the Power Function

Power

Definition 16.9 — Power of a Test
$$\pi(\delta) = \inf_{\theta \in \Theta_1} \bigl(1 - \beta_\delta(\theta)\bigr)$$

In this class, the definition is given for completeness and will not be tested on. Power close to 1 is desirable — the test almost always detects a true alternative.

Level

Definition 16.10 — Level and Asymptotic Level
$$\text{A test has level } \alpha \text{ if } \alpha_\delta(\theta) \le \alpha,\; \forall\, \theta \in \Theta_0$$ $$\text{A test has asymptotic level } \alpha \text{ if } \lim_{n\to\infty} \alpha_\delta(\theta) \le \alpha,\; \forall\, \theta \in \Theta_0$$
Remark on Level
If a test has level 5%, then it trivially has level 10% as well. The converse is not true. Level is an upper bound on the Type I error — we want the tightest bound possible to maximise power. Leaving headroom wastes statistical budget.

In general, a test takes the form:

General Form of a Test
$$\delta = \mathbf{1}[T_n > c]$$ for some test statistic $T_n$ and threshold $c \in \mathbb{R}$. The corresponding rejection region is $R_\delta = \{T_n > c\}$.
Example — Coin Flip with Reversed Hypotheses

We flip a coin with $H_0: p \le 1/2$ vs. $H_1: p > 1/2$. By CLT, $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - p)/\sqrt{p(1-p)} \to \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. By Slutsky's theorem, $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - p)/\sqrt{\bar{X}_n(1-\bar{X}_n)} \to \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ regardless of $p$. If $H_0$ is true ($p \le 1/2$), then:

$$\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - 1/2)}{\sqrt{\bar{X}_n(1-\bar{X}_n)}} \le \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - p)}{\sqrt{\bar{X}_n(1-\bar{X}_n)}}$$

The left-hand side should not be too large, so we reject when it is large. The threshold $t$ is found by the same level calculation as before.

Uniformly More Powerful Tests

Test $\delta'$ is uniformly more powerful than $\delta$ if $P_\theta(\delta'=1) \ge P_\theta(\delta=1)$ for all $\theta \in \Theta_1$, at the same level. Uniformly more powerful tests do not always exist — it is possible that test A is better for some values of $\theta \in \Theta_1$ and test B is better for others.

The solid curve is the power function. In Θ₀ (left) it is constrained to stay below α. Its minimum over Θ₁ (right) is the power π(δ). The dashed curve is a uniformly more powerful test — same level, higher detection probability everywhere in Θ₁.

§ 10

Worked Examples — Is the Coin Fair?

A coin is tossed $n$ times. Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Bernoulli}(p)$, $X_i = 1$ = heads. We test $H_0: p = 1/2$ vs. $H_1: p \ne 1/2$. We reject when:

Rejection Criterion
$$\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - 1/2)}{\sqrt{\bar{X}_n(1-\bar{X}_n)}} > q_{1-\alpha/2}$$

Example 1 — 54 Heads out of 80 Flips

Calculation

$\bar{X}_{80} = 54/80 = 0.68$. The test statistic is:

$$\frac{\sqrt{80}(0.68 - 0.5)}{\sqrt{0.68 \times 0.32}} \approx \frac{8.944 \times 0.18}{0.466} \approx 3.45$$

This exceeds 1.96. It seems quite reasonable to reject the hypothesis $p = 0.5$. Getting 54 heads is very unlikely for a fair coin.

Example 2 — A Smaller Deviation

Calculation

In a different trial, the standardised statistic works out to approximately $0.77$. Since $0.77 < 1.96$, it seems impossible to reject significantly the hypothesis $p = 0.5$. We cannot invalidate it — but we can only conclude this, not that the coin is fair.

The Key Principle
The only things we can do are invalidate $H_0$, or say we cannot invalidate it. We cannot validate it. "Failing to reject" is not proof of truth — it is an absence of evidence.

Multiple Valid Tests

In this class, tests often have multiple correct answers. For example, for $H_0: p = 1/3$ vs. $H_1: p \ne 1/3$, define:

Two Valid Test Statistics
$$T_n = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - 1/3)}{\sqrt{1/3 \cdot 2/3}} \qquad S_n = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - 1/3)}{\sqrt{\bar{X}_n(1-\bar{X}_n)}}$$

For $T_n$ we invoke CLT; for $S_n$ we invoke CLT + Slutsky's. Potential tests include $\delta = \mathbf{1}[|T_n| > q_{1-\alpha/2}]$, $\delta = \mathbf{1}[T_n > q_{1-\alpha}]$, $\delta = \mathbf{1}[T_n < -q_{1-\alpha}]$, and their $S_n$ analogues — infinitely many tests! Don't stress over matching one exact answer.

§ 11

The Test Statistic and Choosing the Threshold $c$

A Recipe for Designing Tests

Since the 1970s, everyone has agreed to take $\alpha = 0.05$ for some reason. Here is the systematic recipe:

  1. Suppose that $H_0$ is true.
  2. Find a test statistic such that if $H_0$ is true, we can say something about its (asymptotic) distribution — ideally something pivotal like $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ or $\chi^2_d$.
  3. Reject $H_0$ if the value of the test statistic is not in the typical range of that distribution.
  4. Define the typical range according to the (asymptotic) level $\alpha$ we want to achieve.
Example 18.1 — Full Worked Test (Bernoulli, Asymptotic Level α)

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Bernoulli}(p)$, unknown $p \in (0,1)$. Test $H_0: p = 1/2$ vs. $H_1: p \ne 1/2$ with asymptotic level $\alpha \in (0,1)$.

By CLT: if $H_0$ is true, $\sqrt{n}(1/2)(1/2) \to 1/4$ in the denominator, so:

$$T_n = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\hat{p}_n - 1/2)}{\sqrt{1/2 \cdot 1/2}} = 2\sqrt{n}\,(\hat{p}_n - 1/2) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$$

where $\hat{p}_n$ is the MLE. The test is:

$$\delta_\alpha = \mathbf{1}[|T_n| > q_{1-\alpha/2}]$$

Sanity check: the asymptotic level of $\delta_\alpha$ is $P_{1/2}(|T_n| > q_{1-\alpha/2}) \to \alpha$. ✓

Three Equivalent Tests at the Same Level

Consider three test statistics for the same problem:

Three Tests — Same Asymptotic Level
$$T_n = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\hat{p}_n - 1/2)}{\sqrt{1/2 \cdot 1/2}}, \qquad \delta_\alpha = \mathbf{1}[|T_n| > q_{1-\alpha/2}]$$ $$S_n = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\hat{p}_n - 0.5)}{\sqrt{\hat{p}_n(1-\hat{p}_n)}}, \qquad \delta'_\alpha = \mathbf{1}[|S_n| > q_{1-\alpha/2}]$$ $$U_n = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\hat{p}_n - 1/2)}{\sqrt{1/2 \cdot 1/2}}, \qquad \delta''_\alpha = \mathbf{1}[U_n > q_{1-\alpha}]$$

All three have the same asymptotic level $\alpha$. However, $\delta'_\alpha$ (using $S_n$) has the best power. $\delta''_\alpha$ (one-sided using $U_n$) has the worst Type II error: we can rewrite $U_n$ as:

Decomposition of U_n
$$U_n = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - p)}{\sqrt{\bar{X}_n(1-\bar{X}_n)}} + \frac{\sqrt{n}(p - 1/2)}{\sqrt{\bar{X}_n(1-\bar{X}_n)}}$$

If $p$ is small, the second term is very negative, so $U_n$ will be small and we will not reject — even when the alternative is true.

Two-Sided Threshold
$$P(|Z| > c) = \alpha \iff c = q_{1-\alpha/2}$$ where $q_{1-\alpha/2}$ is the upper $\alpha/2$-quantile of $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

§ 12

The p-Value

Most statistics professors — and even many researchers — do not correctly know what a p-value is. Let us be precise.

Motivation: The Tipping Point

Recall the coin tossed 80 times, obtaining 54 heads. We reject when the standardised statistic exceeds $q_{1-\alpha/2}$. We compute $\bar{X}_n = 54/80 = 0.68$ and obtain a statistic of $\approx 3.45$. This exceeds 1.96, so we reject at $\alpha = 0.1$ or $\alpha = 0.2$. But if we take $\alpha = 0.01$, we no longer reject!

Consider sliding $\alpha$ from 0 to 1:

  • If $\alpha = 1$: the threshold is the 0.5-quantile (median), which is 0 — we always reject.
  • If $\alpha = 0$: the threshold is the 1-quantile (infinite) — we never reject.
  • In between, there is a unique tipping point where the decision flips from "fail to reject" to "reject."
Definition 18.2 — p-Value
The (asymptotic) p-value of a test $\delta_\alpha$ is the smallest (asymptotic) level $\alpha$ at which $\delta_\alpha$ rejects $H_0$. In summary: $$\text{p-value} \le \alpha \iff H_0 \text{ is rejected by } \delta_\alpha \text{ at (asymptotic) level } \alpha$$ The smaller the p-value, the more confidently one can reject $H_0$. The p-value is a statistic — and therefore a random variable!

Computing the p-Value

For a two-sided test of the form $\delta_\alpha = \mathbf{1}[|T_n| > q_{1-\alpha/2}]$, the p-value is the value of $\alpha$ such that $|T_n| = q_{1-\alpha/2}$:

p-Value Formula (Two-Sided Gaussian Test)
$$|T_n| = q_{1-\alpha/2} \iff \Phi(|T_n|) = 1 - \alpha/2 \iff \alpha = 2 - 2\Phi(|T_n|)$$ $$\boxed{\text{p-value} = 2\bigl(1 - \Phi(|T_n|)\bigr)}$$

For the one-sided test $\delta''_\alpha = \mathbf{1}[U_n > q_{1-\alpha}]$, the p-value is $1 - \Phi(U_n)$.

Golden Rule
$$\text{Reject } H_0 \iff \text{p-value} < \alpha$$ $$\text{Fail to reject } H_0 \iff \text{p-value} \ge \alpha$$

Worked Examples

Coin Flip — 54 heads, T ≈ 3.45
$$\text{p-value} = 2(1-\Phi(3.45)) \approx 2 \times 0.00028 = 0.00057 = 0.057\%$$ Since $0.057\% < 5\%$, we reject at the 5% level. Strong evidence against $H_0$.
Smaller Deviation — T ≈ 0.77
$$\text{p-value} = 2(1-\Phi(0.77)) \approx 2 \times 0.220 = 0.441 = 44.1\%$$ Since $44.1\% > 5\%$, we fail to reject at any typical level. Very weak evidence against $H_0$.

The p-Value Is a Random Variable

Before seeing data, $T_n$ is random, so the p-value (a function of $T_n$) is also random. Under $H_0$, the p-value is uniformly distributed on $[0,1]$. This means that even under a true null, 5% of experiments will yield p-value $< 0.05$ purely by chance — which is exactly the definition of level $\alpha$.

Common Misconception
The p-value is NOT the probability that $H_0$ is true. It is computed assuming $H_0$ is true and measures how surprising the data would be under that assumption.

§ 13

p-Hacking and Threats to Validity

Every guarantee of hypothesis testing rests on three assumptions: (1) $H_0$ and $H_1$ are fixed before seeing the data, (2) the test procedure is chosen before seeing the data, and (3) the test is run once on pre-specified data. Violating any of these is p-hacking and inflates the true Type I error far above $\alpha$.

Concrete Forms of p-Hacking

  • Choosing the alternative after seeing data: If $\bar{X}_{30} = 0.433 < 0.5$, you declare $H_1: p < 0.5$ (one-sided), halving the p-value. But you chose this direction only because the data pointed left. Cheating.
  • Multiple testing without correction: Run 100 tests simultaneously — about 5 will give p-value $< 0.05$ by chance alone. Reporting only those 5 as "discoveries" is dishonest.
  • Optional stopping: Continuously check p-values and stop the moment significance is first reached.
  • Hypothesis switching: Pre-register one endpoint, then report a different one that turned out significant.
The Replication Crisis
Fields like psychology and certain areas of biomedicine have faced a "replication crisis" — many published findings could not be reproduced by independent labs. A leading cause is p-hacking combined with publication bias (journals favouring p < 0.05 results). Some psychology and psychometrics journals have banned p-values entirely from their publications.
§ 14

The Neyman–Pearson Paradigm

For given hypotheses, among all tests of (asymptotic) level $\alpha$, is it possible to find the one with maximal power? According to Neyman-Pearson, it is possible to find the most powerful test with a given level — but in this class, we study only a few specific cases.

The three tests $\delta_\alpha$, $\delta'_\alpha$, $\delta''_\alpha$ from §11 all have equivalent asymptotic level, but $\delta''_\alpha$ had terrible power and $\delta'_\alpha$ had the highest power. Furthermore, the trivial test $\delta \equiv 0$ has perfect level but no power.

The Chi-Squared Distribution $\chi^2_d$

Definition 18.3 — Chi-Squared Distribution
For a positive integer $d$, the $\chi^2$ distribution with $d$ degrees of freedom is the law of the random variable: $$Z_1^2 + Z_2^2 + \cdots + Z_d^2$$ where $Z_1, \ldots, Z_d \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

Key Properties

  • If $Z \sim \mathcal{N}_d(0, I_d)$, then $\|Z\|^2 \sim \chi^2_d$. This is because each dimension is independent of every other.
  • $\mathbb{E}[\chi^2_d] = d$, $\text{Var}(\chi^2_d) = 2d$. Support is $(0, \infty)$.
  • Special case: $\chi^2_2 \sim \text{Exponential}(1/2)$. Verifiable directly from the PDF.
  • Cochran's theorem: for $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, if $\tilde{S}_n$ is the unbiased sample variance, then $(n-1)\tilde{S}_n / \sigma^2 \sim \chi^2_{n-1}$.

Geometric Intuition — Throwing Darts at a Target

Imagine throwing a dart where horizontal deviation $Z_1 \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and vertical deviation $Z_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, independently. The squared distance from the centre is $D^2 = Z_1^2 + Z_2^2 \sim \chi^2_2$. This is the core reason chi-squared arises so naturally: any time you measure the squared Euclidean distance of a Gaussian vector from the origin, you get chi-squared. Pythagoras is literally why chi-squared exists.

Why Chi-Squared Tables Need Many Rows

Unlike $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ (one distribution, one table), there is a fundamentally different chi-squared distribution for each $d$, and there is no standardisation that collapses all degrees of freedom to a common scale. The shape itself changes with $d$, so chi-squared tables list separate entries for each degree of freedom.

Student's t-Distribution

Remark 21.6 — On the Name "Student"
"Student" is not a researcher — it is a pseudonym. An intern (William Sealy Gosset) was working with confidential data at the Guinness Brewery and was forbidden from publishing under his real name. He published anonymously under "Student." This is why it is called Student's t-distribution.
Definition 21.7 — Student's t-Distribution
For $d \in \mathbb{N}$, the Student's distribution with $d$ degrees of freedom (denoted $t_d$) is the law of: $$T = \frac{U}{\sqrt{V/d}}$$ where $U \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, $V \sim \chi^2_d$, and $U \perp V$.
Theorem 21.8 — Cochran's Theorem (Student's form)
For $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, if $\tilde{S}_n$ is the unbiased sample variance, then: $$\frac{\sqrt{n-1}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sqrt{\tilde{S}_n}} \sim t_{n-1}$$ Equivalently, since $(n-1)\tilde{S}_n / \sigma^2 \sim \chi^2_{n-1}$ and $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)/\sigma \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, and these are independent: $$\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sqrt{\tilde{S}_n}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{n/(n-1)}} = \frac{\sqrt{n-1}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sqrt{\tilde{S}_n}} \sim t_{n-1}$$

Remarks

  • This is really unusual: it suggests that $\hat{\mu} \perp \hat{\sigma}^2$ if the $X_i$ are Gaussian. That is definitely not true in general (for Poisson, Bernoulli, etc.).
  • We can rescale to get $n\tilde{S}_n/\sigma^2 \sim \chi^2_{n-1}$.
  • The t-distribution has heavier tails than $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$, reflecting extra uncertainty from estimating $\sigma^2$.
  • As $d \to \infty$: $t_d \to \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. For $d \ge 50$: practically indistinguishable from normal.
Example 21.9 — Non-Asymptotic Test for Gaussian Mean

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$. Test $H_0: \mu = \mu_0$ vs. $H_1: \mu \ne \mu_0$. If $H_0$ is true:

$$T_n = \frac{\sqrt{n-1}(\bar{X}_n - \mu_0)}{\sqrt{\tilde{S}_n}} \sim t_{n-1}$$

This is exact even when $n$ is small — it does not "converge in distribution"; it is a $t_{n-1}$ random variable. The test is $\delta = \mathbf{1}[|T_n| > q_{1-\alpha}]$, where $q_{1-\alpha}$ is the $(1-\alpha)$-quantile of $t_{n-1}$.

So why is this "useless"? It assumes the sample is perfectly Gaussian. Cochran's theorem breaks down even if the sample is only slightly non-Gaussian. In practice, you will never get perfect random variables, so this result, while elegant, has limited practical scope.

Wald's Test for Multi-Dimensional Parameters

Consider an i.i.d. sample $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ with statistical model $(\mathcal{E}, \{P_\theta\}_{\theta \in \Theta})$, where $\Theta \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d$, $d \ge 1$, and let $\theta_0 \in \Theta$ be fixed and given. Consider:

Hypotheses for Wald's Test
$$H_0: \theta = \theta_0 \qquad H_1: \theta \ne \theta_0$$

Let $\hat{\theta}^{\text{MLE}}$ be the MLE. Under the standard technical conditions, $\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta} - \theta) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \mathcal{I}(\theta)^{-1})$. If $H_0$ is true, substitute $\theta_0$:

Asymptotic Normality under H₀
$$\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta} - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \mathcal{I}(\theta_0)^{-1})$$

Deriving the Chi-Squared Statistic

Since $\mathcal{I}(\theta)$ is positive semi-definite (it is the covariance matrix of the score), we can define $\mathcal{I}(\theta)^{1/2}$. Setting $A = \mathcal{I}(\theta_0)^{1/2}$ in the multivariate CLT:

Standardised Vector → Standard Gaussian
$$\sqrt{n}\,\mathcal{I}(\theta_0)^{1/2}(\hat{\theta} - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} z,\quad z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_d)$$

Taking the squared norm (a continuous function), using $\|u\|^2 = u^\top u$:

Squared Norm → Chi-Squared
$$n(\hat{\theta}-\theta_0)^\top \mathcal{I}(\theta_0)(\hat{\theta}-\theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_d$$
Theorem 19.2 — Wald's Test
Let $T_n = n(\hat{\theta} - \theta_0)^\top \mathcal{I}(\hat{\theta})(\hat{\theta} - \theta_0)$. Wald's test with asymptotic level $\alpha \in (0,1)$ is: $$\delta = \mathbf{1}[T_n > q_{1-\alpha}]$$ where $q_{1-\alpha}$ is the $(1-\alpha)$ quantile of $\chi^2_d$ (use tables). We replace $\mathcal{I}(\theta_0)$ with $\mathcal{I}(\hat{\theta})$ via Slutsky's theorem.
  • If $H_1$ is of the form $\theta \ne \theta_0$, Wald's test is the most powerful.
  • If $H_1$ is of the form $\theta > \theta_0$, it is not the most powerful.

Geometric Intuition

The rejection region can be visualised as a ball of radius $\sqrt{q_{1-\alpha}}$ in the geometry defined by $\mathcal{I}(\theta_0)$. Directions of high Fisher information (high precision) have small radii — even small deviations are considered significant. Directions of low information tolerate larger deviations.

Reduction to 1D

For $d = 1$: $T_n = n(\hat{\theta} - \theta_0)^2 \mathcal{I}(\hat{\theta})$, and $T_n > q_{1-\alpha}(\chi^2_1)$ is equivalent to $|\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}-\theta_0)\sqrt{\mathcal{I}(\hat\theta)}| > q_{1-\alpha/2}(\mathcal{N}(0,1))$ — exactly the standard z-test. Wald's test generalises the familiar z-test to $d$ dimensions.

§ 18

The Likelihood Ratio Test

Note (from April 12 Lecture)
We can apply the likelihood ratio test wherever we can apply Wald's test, since "all parameters" is a specific case of "some parameters" — simply set $r = 0$.

Consider an i.i.d. sample with model $(\mathcal{E}, \{P_\theta\}_{\theta \in \Theta})$, $\Theta \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d$, $d \ge 1$. Suppose the null hypothesis has the form:

Constrained Null Hypothesis
$$H_0: (\theta_{r+1}, \ldots, \theta_d) = (\theta^{(0)}_{r+1}, \ldots, \theta^{(0)}_d)$$

where we only test some parameters. Let $\hat{\theta}_n = \arg\max_{\theta \in \Theta} \ell_n(\theta)$ be the (unconstrained) MLE and let $\hat{\theta}_n^c = \arg\max_{\theta \in \Theta_0} \ell_n(\theta)$ be the constrained MLE.

Example 19.3 — Testing the Variance
Consider $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, $\mu, \sigma^2$ unknown. Test $H_0: \sigma^2 = 1$ vs. $H_1: \sigma^2 \ne 1$. Here $d = 2$, $r = 1$ since $\theta = (\mu, \sigma^2)$ is two-dimensional and we constrain one parameter.
Example 19.4 — Testing the Mean (Explicit Constrained MLE)

Same setup: $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$. Test $H_0: \mu = 0$ vs. $H_1: \mu \ne 0$.

The (unconstrained) MLE is $\hat{\theta} = (\hat{\mu}, \hat{\sigma}^2) = (\bar{X}_n, \bar{X_n^2} - \bar{X}_n^2)$.

For the constrained MLE (set $\mu = 0$):

$$\log L_n(0, \sigma^2) = -\frac{n}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_i X_i^2$$

Maximising over $\sigma^2$ gives $(\hat{\sigma}^2)^c = \overline{X^2}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i X_i^2$.

LRT Statistic
$$T_n = 2\bigl[\ell_n(\hat{\theta}_n) - \ell_n(\hat{\theta}_n^c)\bigr] \ge 0$$
Theorem 19.5 — Wilks' Theorem
Assume $H_0$ is true and the MLE technical conditions are satisfied. Then: $$T_n \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{d-r}$$ The likelihood ratio test with asymptotic level $\alpha \in (0,1)$ is $\mathbf{1}[T_n > q_{1-\alpha}]$, where $q_{1-\alpha}$ is the $(1-\alpha)$-quantile of $\chi^2_{d-r}$. The asymptotic p-value is $1 - F_{d-r}(T_n)$, where $F_{d-r}$ is the CDF of $\chi^2_{d-r}$. The same holds for Wald's test.

Intuition

  • If H₀ true: constrained and unconstrained MLEs are similar; $T_n \approx 0$ → fail to reject.
  • If H₀ false: unconstrained MLE is pulled away from $\Theta_0$; $T_n$ is large → reject.
§ 19

Testing Functions of Parameters — Implicit Hypotheses

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be i.i.d. and let $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^d$. Let $g: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^k$ be continuously differentiable, with $k < d$ (otherwise the system is over-specified). Consider:

Implicit Hypothesis
$$H_0: g(\theta) = 0 \qquad H_1: g(\theta) \ne 0$$
Example 21.2 — Forms of g
  • $g(\theta) = \theta_1 - \theta_2$: one-dimensional, tests whether $\theta_1 = \theta_2$.
  • $g(\theta) = (\theta_1, \theta_2)$: provides a test about the first two coordinates.
  • $g(\theta) = (\theta_1, \theta_3 - \theta_2)$: tests whether $\theta_1 = 0$ and $\theta_2 = \theta_3$.
Where else have we seen functions like $g$? The Delta method!

Applying the Delta Method

Suppose an asymptotically normal estimator $\hat{\theta}_n$ is available: $\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}_d(0, \Sigma(\theta))$. By the multivariate Delta method:

Multivariate Delta Method
$$\sqrt{n}\bigl(g(\hat{\theta}_n) - g(\theta)\bigr) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}_k(0, \Gamma(\theta))$$ where $\Gamma(\theta) = \nabla g(\theta)^\top \Sigma(\theta)\, \nabla g(\theta) \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times k}$.

By Slutsky, replace $\Gamma(\theta)$ with $\Gamma(\hat{\theta})$. If $H_0$ is true ($g(\theta) = 0$):

Delta Method Test Statistic
$$T_n = n\,g(\hat{\theta}_n)^\top \Gamma^{-1}(\hat{\theta}_n)\,g(\hat{\theta}_n) \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_k \quad \text{under } H_0$$

The p-value is $1 - F_k(T_n)$, where $F_k$ is the CDF of $\chi^2_k$. As usual, the test is $\mathbf{1}[T_n > q_{1-\alpha}]$ where $q_{1-\alpha}$ is the $(1-\alpha)$-quantile of $\chi^2_k$.

Why Slutsky Works Here — A Subtle Point

We replace $\Gamma(\theta)$ with $\Gamma(\hat{\theta})$ via Slutsky. Crucially, we do not need to invert $g$ to get the null value of $\theta$ — $g$ may not be invertible, and $g^{-1}(0)$ could be a complicated manifold. Slutsky lets us bypass this entirely.

§ 20

Multinomial Chi-Squared Test

Setup

Let $\mathcal{E} = \{a_1, \ldots, a_K\}$ be a finite sample space and let $(\mathbb{P}_p)_{p \in \Delta_K}$ be the family of all probability distributions on $\mathcal{E}$:

Simplex of Probability Vectors
$$\Delta_K = \left\{p = (p_1, \ldots, p_K) \in (0,1)^K : \sum_{j=1}^K p_j = 1\right\}$$

For $p \in \Delta_K$ and $X \sim \mathbb{P}_p$: $\mathbb{P}_p(X = a_j) = p_j$, $j = 1, \ldots, K$. The Bernoulli distribution is a special case where $K = 2$.

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathbb{P}_p$ for some unknown $p \in \Delta_K$, and let $p^0 \in \Delta_K$ be fixed. We want to test:

Multinomial Hypotheses
$$H_0: p = p^0 \qquad H_1: p \ne p^0$$ with asymptotic level $\alpha \in (0,1)$.
Example 21.4 — Testing Uniformity
If we let $p^0 = (1/K, 1/K, \ldots, 1/K)$, then we are testing whether $\mathbb{P}_p$ is the uniform distribution on $\mathcal{E}$.

The MLE and the Problem with Wald's Test

The likelihood of the model is:

Multinomial Likelihood
$$L_n(X_1,\ldots,X_n; p) = p_1^{N_1} p_2^{N_2} \cdots p_K^{N_K}$$ where $N_j = \#\{i = 1,\ldots,n : X_i = a_j\}$.

The MLE is $\hat{p}_j = N_j/n$ — the sample proportion of category $j$, subject to the constraint $\sum_j p_j = 1$.

We might try to apply the CLT: $\sqrt{n}(\hat{p} - p) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}_K(0, \Sigma(p))$. However, $\Sigma(p)$ is not invertible! The proportions must sum to 1, so the vector $\hat{p} - p$ lives in a $(K-1)$-dimensional subspace — the covariance matrix is singular, and Wald's test cannot be applied directly.

The Chi-Squared Test Statistic

It is possible to find a matrix $A(p)$ such that after multiplication, one obtains almost the identity (with a zero in the last entry), and the squared norm converges to $\chi^2_{K-1}$. The result is:

Theorem 21.5 — Multinomial Chi-Squared Statistic
$$T_n = n\sum_{j=1}^K \frac{(\hat{p}_j - p^0_j)^2}{p^0_j} \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{K-1}$$ This formula should go on your equation sheet — it is important but not very intuitive. Wald's test is more intuitive; you should know both.
Chi-Squared Test and p-Value
$$\delta_\alpha = \mathbf{1}[T_n > q_{1-\alpha}]$$ where $q_{1-\alpha}$ is the $(1-\alpha)$-quantile of $\chi^2_{K-1}$. The asymptotic p-value is $1 - F_{K-1}(T_n)$, where $F_{K-1}$ is the CDF of $\chi^2_{K-1}$.
Why K−1 Degrees of Freedom?
The $K$ proportions must sum to 1, so there are only $K-1$ free parameters. Equivalently, the constraint $\sum_j p_j = 1$ removes one dimension from the problem. The degrees of freedom reflect the effective dimension of the parameter space being tested — exactly as in the LRT (where degrees of freedom count the number of constrained parameters).
§ 21

Glossary of Key Terms

Null Hypothesis H₀

Default/status quo assumption. Assumed true until sufficient evidence against it. We never "prove H₀ true" — only "fail to reject."

Alternative Hypothesis H₁

Scientific claim to establish. Rejecting H₀ provides evidence for H₁. In this class, we do not directly control what happens under H₁.

Test δ

Statistic taking values 0 (fail to reject H₀) or 1 (reject H₀). Equivalently, the indicator of the rejection region.

Statistic

Measurable function of the data alone — computable from observations without knowing any unknown parameters.

Rejection Region R_δ

Set of data samples for which δ = 1. Complement is the acceptance region. For x = (x₁,…,xₙ): R_δ = {x ∈ Eⁿ : δ(x) = 1}.

Test Statistic T_n

Scalar (or vector) summary of data, pivotal under H₀, compared to threshold c. Large values indicate evidence against H₀.

Pivotal Distribution

A distribution that does not depend on any unknown parameter under H₀. Essential for computing critical values. Examples: N(0,1), χ²_d, t_d.

Type I Error (α_δ)

Rejecting H₀ when it is true: α_δ(θ) = P_θ(δ=1) for θ ∈ Θ₀. Function of θ, not a single number. Analogy: convicting an innocent person.

Type II Error (β_δ)

Failing to reject H₀ when H₁ is true: β_δ(θ) = P_θ(δ=0) for θ ∈ Θ₁. Function of θ. Analogy: acquitting a guilty person.

Level α

A test has level α if α_δ(θ) ≤ α for all θ ∈ Θ₀. A test of level 5% also has level 10%. Asymptotic level uses the limit n→∞.

Power π(δ)

Minimum detection probability over Θ₁: π(δ) = inf_{Θ₁}(1−β_δ(θ)). Equals 1 minus worst-case Type II error. Higher is better.

p-Value

Smallest (asymptotic) level α at which the test rejects given the observed data. Equals 2(1−Φ(|T_n|)) for two-sided Gaussian tests. Uniform on [0,1] under H₀. A random variable.

Golden Rule

Reject H₀ iff p-value < α. Fail to reject iff p-value ≥ α.

Neyman–Pearson Paradigm

Fix Type I error at α; among all such tests, maximise power. The dominant frequentist framework. Since the 1970s, α = 0.05 is the conventional standard.

p-Hacking

Choosing hypotheses, stopping rules, or analyses after seeing data to produce smaller p-values. Inflates true Type I error far above nominal α. Invalidates all guarantees.

Chi-Squared χ²_d

Sum of d independent N(0,1)² variables. Mean = d, Var = 2d. Support (0,∞). Special case: χ²_2 = Exp(1/2). Cochran's: (n−1)S̃_n/σ² ~ χ²_{n−1}.

Degrees of Freedom

Parameter of χ² and t distributions. For sample variance: n−1 (one used by sample mean). For LRT: number of constrained parameters (d−r). For multinomial: K−1.

Cochran's Theorem

For Gaussian data: (n−1)S̃_n/σ² ~ χ²_{n−1} and S̃_n ⊥ X̄_n. The −1 arises because deviations from sample mean sum to zero — one degree of freedom is "used up."

Student's t_d

Z/√(V/d) with Z~N(0,1), V~χ²_d, independent. Heavier tails than normal. Invented by Gosset at Guinness Brewery (1908, alias "Student"). Converges to N(0,1) as d→∞.

Wald's Test

T_n = n(θ̂−θ₀)ᵀ 𝒮(θ̂)(θ̂−θ₀) ~ χ²_d under H₀. Measures distance in Fisher-information geometry. Most powerful for H₁: θ ≠ θ₀.

Likelihood Ratio Test

T_n = 2[ℓ(θ̂_mle) − ℓ(θ̂_constrained)] ~ χ²_{d−r} under H₀ (Wilks). Compares unconstrained vs. constrained model fit.

Constrained MLE θ̂ᶜ

MLE maximised only over Θ₀ (the null parameter space). Always: ℓ(θ̂_mle) ≥ ℓ(θ̂ᶜ) since we maximise over a larger set.

Delta Method Test (Implicit)

For H₀: g(θ)=0, test statistic T_n = n·g(θ̂)ᵀΓ⁻¹(θ̂)g(θ̂) ~ χ²_k. Γ(θ) = ∇g(θ)ᵀΣ(θ)∇g(θ). Generalises Wald's test to smooth functions of θ.

Multinomial χ² Test

For H₀: p = p⁰ on K categories, T_n = n∑(p̂_j − p⁰_j)²/p⁰_j ~ χ²_{K−1}. Singular covariance of sample proportions prevents Wald's test; this formula accounts for the constraint ∑p_j = 1.
