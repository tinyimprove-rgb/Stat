§ 01

Why Hypothesis Testing? Estimation vs. Testing

Up to this point the course focused on estimation: deriving the best numerical guess for an unknown parameter \(\theta\). Maximum likelihood estimation was the central tool. In many cases — particularly for Gaussian models — the MLE turned out simply to be the sample average. Even when we already knew the answer intuitively, going through MLE confirmed the intuition and gave us tools to analyse the estimator's quality using the Central Limit Theorem.

Now we introduce a fundamentally different kind of question. Instead of asking "what is \(\mu\)?" we ask a yes/no question: "Is \(\mu\) equal to some reference value, or has it changed?" This is parametric hypothesis testing — "parametric" because the claim is about a parameter of a known distributional family.

Key Shift in Perspective
In estimation, randomness (via the CLT) appeared only in the analysis of the estimator after it was designed. In testing, randomness enters both the design and the analysis. We must account for random fluctuations in order to build the test itself — not just to understand it afterwards. The CLT is no longer just a quality certificate; it is part of the test construction.

Hypothesis testing is the foundation of essentially all data-driven scientific inquiry. Any time you formulate a scientific claim and try to confirm or deny it with data, you are implicitly doing a hypothesis test:

  • "Knocking down this gene cures melanoma." → collect patient outcomes → yes/no answer.
  • "This drug reduces coughing." → clinical trial → yes/no answer.
  • "Runners were faster this year." → sample race times → yes/no answer.

Everyone has heard of hypothesis testing; here we make it precise. The three goals of statistical inference are: estimation (produces an approximate value), confidence intervals (find a range), and hypothesis tests (answer a yes/no question — for example, "is it true that \(\theta_1\) and \(\theta_2\) are significantly different?"). This chapter focuses on the last.

§ 02

Motivating Example — The Cherry Blossom Run

The Credit Union Cherry Blossom Run is a 10-mile race held annually in Washington D.C. The 2009 and 2012 datasets are both publicly available in R.

Known Data from 2009 (Full Population, N ≈ 15,000)
  • Mean running time: \(\mu_{2009} = 103.5\) minutes
  • Variance: \(\sigma^2_{2009} = 373\)
  • Distribution: histogram of all 15,000 times closely matches a Gaussian bell curve

The Problem

Suppose our budget allows surveying only \(n = 10\) runners from the 2012 race. We want to know: were runners faster in 2012 than in 2009? How can we make a valid statistical claim from just 10 observations?

Where Randomness Comes From

We select \(n\) runners uniformly at random. Each runner is an elementary outcome \(\omega\) in the probability space — the runners are the omegas. The random variable of interest is their running time. This is how we inject randomness: not from the measurement itself, but from the random selection of who we observe.

From a given runner, many variables could be measured: country of origin, age, height. Here we care only about running time: \(X_i = \text{running time of runner } i\).

Why the Gaussian Assumption Is Justified

The 2009 histogram is well-approximated by a Gaussian. This is expected: running time is the cumulative result of many small independent factors (physiological, environmental, psychological) — exactly the CLT setting. Bell-shaped distributions arise everywhere in nature for this reason. We therefore assume \(X_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)\) for the 2012 data, with \(\sigma^2 = 373\) fixed (same as 2009) and \(\mu\) unknown.

Caveat on the Variance Assumption
Fixing \(\sigma^2_{2012} = 373\) is acknowledged to be incorrect — the actual 2012 data has a different variance. This assumption is made for simplicity of exposition. The Gaussian shape assumption, by contrast, is quite robust: the CLT guarantees it arises whenever you aggregate many small independent effects, and this is hard to violate across years for the same race.

The Statistical Question

Cherry Blossom Hypothesis
\[H_0: \mu = 103.5 \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1: \mu < 103.5\]

We use a one-sided alternative because we ask specifically whether runners were faster (smaller time), not just different. This concentration of the alternative on one side increases statistical power.

§ 03

Two-Sample Test — Clinical Drug Trial

This is the more realistic scenario: no fixed reference value exists. Pharmaceutical companies face this problem daily. The FDA requires rigorously controlled trials before approving any drug.

Setup

A new cough syrup is tested against a placebo. Patients are randomly split:

  • Control group (\(n_{\text{ctrl}}\) patients): receives maple syrup (placebo). Expected coughs/hour: \(\mu_{\text{ctrl}}\).
  • Drug group (\(n_{\text{drug}}\) patients): receives the real drug. Expected coughs/hour: \(\mu_{\text{drug}}\).

Note that maple syrup itself has a soothing effect on coughing — it is sweet and helps slightly. The placebo effect is real but unknown. We cannot simply compare against zero; we must measure the placebo effect too.

Modeling Assumption
Cough counts are modeled as Poisson(\(\mu\)): non-negative integers, fully characterised by their mean. This is natural for count data. So there is one unknown parameter per group.
Drug Trial Hypotheses
\[H_0: \mu_{\text{drug}} = \mu_{\text{ctrl}} \quad \text{vs.} \quad H_1: \mu_{\text{drug}} < \mu_{\text{ctrl}}\]

Two Sources of Randomness

Unlike the Cherry Blossom case (one unknown vs. a known reference), here both means are estimated:

  • \(\bar{X}^{\text{drug}}\) estimates \(\mu_{\text{drug}}\) with fluctuation scale \(1/\sqrt{n_{\text{drug}}}\)
  • \(\bar{X}^{\text{ctrl}}\) estimates \(\mu_{\text{ctrl}}\) with fluctuation scale \(1/\sqrt{n_{\text{ctrl}}}\)

Any buffer in the test must account for both sources. The difference \(\bar{X}^{\text{drug}} - \bar{X}^{\text{ctrl}}\) is approximately \(\mathcal{N}(\mu_{\text{drug}} - \mu_{\text{ctrl}}, \sigma^2_{\text{combined}})\) and under \(H_0\) is centred at zero.

Why We Make It Hard to Conclude the Drug Works

If \(\mu_{\text{drug}} = \mu_{\text{ctrl}}\) (the drug is just corn syrup), the difference \(\bar{X}^{\text{drug}} - \bar{X}^{\text{ctrl}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)\). Without any buffer, this quantity is negative 50% of the time, meaning a naive test would approve 50% of all completely ineffective drugs. We demand the difference be substantially more negative than random chance could explain — far into the left tail of the null distribution.

Unequal Group Sizes

The two groups need not be the same size. This arises because:

  • Patient eligibility and dropouts are unpredictable
  • Ethical pressure: desperate patients prefer the real drug over placebo
  • Variance optimisation: if one group has higher variance, you want more subjects there

This is studied formally in the field of Design of Experiments.

§ 04

The Role of Modeling Assumptions

Modeling assumptions are not just conveniences — they are essential for hypothesis testing to work.

Why Restricting the Distribution Space Is Necessary

Consider the universe of all possible probability distributions. Without constraints, data will always find some distribution that fits better than any specific null. For example:

Why Overfitting Destroys Testing
Suppose you collect 10 running times: \(\{91, 95, 102, \ldots\}\). The most likely distribution over all possible distributions is the discrete one placing mass \(1/10\) on each observed value. This fits perfectly. You would always reject any Gaussian null — not because the data is non-Gaussian, but because you are searching across an enormous space. Any finite sample fits some exotic distribution perfectly by chance.

How Assumptions Sequentially Reduce the Problem

For the Cherry Blossom test, each assumption removes a dimension of uncertainty:

  1. Assume Gaussian: eliminates all non-Gaussian families (exponential, Poisson, discrete, etc.).
  2. Fix \(\sigma^2 = 373\): reduces to a one-parameter family indexed by \(\mu\) alone.
  3. One-sided alternative \(\mu < 103.5\): removes the possibility \(\mu > 103.5\) from consideration, focusing all power on detecting improvements.
The Fundamental Trade-off in Assumptions
More assumptions → more focused, more powerful test, but conclusions are more sensitive to those assumptions being correct. Fewer assumptions → more robust, but lower power. Good statistical practice means making the minimum set of defensible assumptions required to answer the specific question.
§ 05

Formal Hypothesis Formulation

Let \(X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} P_\theta\) where \(\theta \in \Theta\). Let \(\Theta_0\) and \(\Theta_1\) be two disjoint subsets of \(\Theta\). Consider the two hypotheses:

Hypothesis Testing Problem
\[H_0 : \theta \in \Theta_0 \quad \text{(null hypothesis)}\] \[H_1 : \theta \in \Theta_1 \quad \text{(alternative hypothesis)}\] \[\Theta_0 \cap \Theta_1 = \emptyset, \quad \Theta_0, \Theta_1 \subseteq \Theta\]

We will always either be able to invalidate \(H_0\) (reject it), or conclude that we cannot invalidate it. We never "accept \(H_0\)" — the only options are to reject or fail to reject. In this class, we do not care about \(H_1\) directly: we focus entirely on controlling what happens under \(H_0\).

Note that \(\Theta_0 \cup \Theta_1\) need not equal all of \(\Theta\) — there can be parameter values neither hypothesis covers (e.g., \(\mu > 103.5\) in the Cherry Blossom example).

Example 16.1 — Fair Coin
Suppose \(X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Bernoulli}(p)\). We would like to test if \(p = 1/2\). The hypotheses are: \[H_0: p = 1/2 \qquad H_1: p \ne 1/2\] and the parameter sets are: \[\Theta_0 = \{1/2\} \qquad \Theta_1 = (0,1) \setminus \{1/2\}\]
Example — One-Sided Coin (Adversarial Setting)
Now suppose we want to know if the coin leans either way, and the professor wins if the coin is \(p \ge 1/2\). The hypotheses are: \[H_0: p \ge 1/2 \qquad H_1: p < 1/2\] and the parameter sets are \(\Theta_0 = [1/2, 1)\) and \(\Theta_1 = (0, 1/2)\).
Example — Smart Opponent
Suppose our professor is a smart opponent who would never play with a coin for which \(p < 1/2\). More reasonable hypotheses are: \[H_0: p = 1/2 \qquad H_1: p < 1/2\] with \(\Theta_0 = \{1/2\}\) and \(\Theta_1 = (0, 1/2)\). These do not cover the whole parameter space (since \(p > 1/2\) is excluded) — but that is fine.
Example 16.2 — HIV Testing

People with HIV have extra antibodies, so we test by checking antibody concentration. We run many diluted samples, where each \(X_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)\), and check if \(\mu > \mu_0\) (our threshold). Suppose \(X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)\) where \(\mu\) represents antibody concentration. The hypotheses are:

\[H_0: \mu \ge \mu_0 \qquad H_1: \mu < \mu_0\] and the parameter sets are: \[\Theta_0 = [\mu_0, \infty) \times (0, \infty) \qquad \Theta_1 = (-\infty, \mu_0) \times (0, \infty)\]

Don't forget to include \(\sigma^2\) in the parameter space!

There are two types of errors. A false negative means the patient is sick and we send them home — this is VERY dangerous. A false positive means the patient is fine and we treat them — not great, but not fatal. The null hypothesis should therefore be "the patient is sick," since we do not want to mistakenly reject this.

Asymmetry Between \(H_0\) and \(H_1\)

  • \(H_0\) is the conservative default position. We require evidence to overcome it.
  • If data is inconclusive, we side with \(H_0\) — not because it is true, but because we lack evidence against it.
  • We never "accept \(H_0\)." We can only fail to reject it.
  • \(H_1\) is the scientific claim — a discovery, an improvement, a new effect.
  • There is inherent asymmetry: in the case that \(H_0\) is true, it is very dangerous or costly to reject it. This determines the assignment of \(H_0\) and \(H_1\).
The Use of H₁
Question: What is the use of defining \(H_1\) if we know nothing about it? Short answer: no use in this class. Long answer: it would still be ideal to control false positives (patient is healthy and we treat them) since there may be nontrivial costs. However, false negatives are much more dangerous, so we focus on them in this class. In advanced settings, \(H_1\) matters for computing power.
The "Innocent Until Proven Guilty" Analogy
H₀ = innocent. The prosecution must bring enough evidence to convince the jury. Without sufficient evidence, the verdict is "not guilty" — which is not the same as "innocent." O.J. Simpson was found not guilty
