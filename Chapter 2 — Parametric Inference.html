§1 Motivation for Statistical Modeling

Suppose you collect a dataset — for instance, the number of siblings (including oneself) reported by college students. The raw data is a list of positive integers. How should we make sense of it?

"All models are wrong, but some of them are useful." — Attributed to George Box

One naive approach is to record the exact fraction of observations equal to each value. But this is inefficient: with a small sample, rare values receive zero probability mass even when they almost certainly exist in the population. The remedy is statistical modeling: choosing a structured family of distributions that compresses information, enables inference, and generalizes beyond the observed data.

The Modeling Trade-off

A good model must satisfy two competing demands:

  • Rich enough to plausibly contain the true distribution generating the data.
  • Simple enough (few parameters) that those parameters can be estimated accurately from a finite sample.
Key Intuition

Injecting prior knowledge into a model reduces the work the data must do. Fewer parameters to estimate means more accurate estimates from the same sample size. The fewer the degrees of freedom you leave to chance, the better your estimators can perform.

Worked Example: Number of Siblings

Observed integers $\{1, 2, 3, \ldots, 7^+\}$ suggest seven free probabilities to estimate — but a sample of 20 students will likely assign zero probability to families of 4 or more simply by chance, not because such families do not exist. By fitting a Poisson distribution with a single parameter $\lambda$, we:

  • Reduce the estimation problem from 7 parameters to 1.
  • Guarantee that every integer value receives a strictly positive probability (since $e^{-\lambda} > 0$).
  • Use information from small values to constrain estimates at large values.

§2 The Standard Statistical Setup

Throughout this course, the standard assumption is:

Core Assumption

Observations $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) with some unknown distribution $P$.

Written: $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} P$

Why i.i.d.? Independence enables us to combine observations (take averages, apply the Law of Large Numbers). Identical distribution ensures all observations are measuring the same underlying phenomenon. Without these, no coherent global inference is possible.

Types of Assumptions on $P$

We can constrain the unknown distribution $P$ at various levels of specificity:

Assumption LevelExampleDegrees of Freedom
No assumptionAny distribution on $\mathbb{R}$Infinite
Density exists$P$ has a pdf $f$Infinite (all densities)
Smooth density$f$ is $k$-times differentiableInfinite, but structured
Parametric family$\text{Poisson}(\lambda)$, $\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)$Finite — 1 or 2 parameters
Known distribution$\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma_0^2)$, $\sigma_0^2$ known1 (only $\mu$ unknown)

§3 Parametric vs. Non-Parametric Models

Parametric Model

A model in which the unknown distribution $P$ is fully described by a finite-dimensional parameter vector $\theta \in \Theta \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d$. This course focuses primarily on the case $d = 1$ (one-dimensional $\theta$).

Non-Parametric Model

A model in which the parameter space is infinite-dimensional — for example, when $P$ is characterized by an unknown density function $f$, and the class of all valid densities is the parameter space. Histograms are the canonical non-parametric estimation tool, as they estimate $f$ without assuming any specific functional form.

Important Distinction

"Non-parametric" does not mean "parameter-free." It means the parameter (e.g., a function) lives in an infinite-dimensional space. In practice, any implementation truncates this space.

§4 Formal Definition: Statistical Experiment

Definition — Statistical Model

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be $n$ i.i.d. random variables in some measurable space $E$ (usually $E \subseteq \mathbb{R}$), with common distribution $\mathbb{P}$. A statistical model associated to this experiment is a pair

$$\bigl(E,\;(\mathbb{P}_\theta)_{\theta \in \Theta}\bigr)$$

where:

  • $E$ is the sample space — the set of values a single observation can take.
  • $(\mathbb{P}_\theta)_{\theta \in \Theta}$ is a family of probability distributions on $E$.
  • $\Theta$ is any set, called the parameter set.

Well-Specified Models

Definition — Well-Specified

A model is well-specified if there exists a true parameter $\theta \in \Theta$ such that $\mathbb{P} = \mathbb{P}_\theta$ — that is, the true data-generating distribution belongs to the assumed model family.

We typically work under the well-specified assumption, while acknowledging (after Box) that no model is perfectly correct. Misspecification is an active research area; for this course, we assume the model is accurate enough to be useful.

The True Parameter

The particular $\theta$ for which $\mathbb{P} = \mathbb{P}_\theta$ is called the true parameter. It is unknown. The aim of the statistical experiment is to estimate $\theta$, or to check properties of $\theta$ when they carry special meaning — for example: Is $\theta > 2$? Is $\theta = 1/2$? Is $\theta \neq 0$?

For this course, we always assume $\Theta \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d$ for some $d \geq 1$ — making the model parametric.

Why Parameters Matter Beyond Distributions

In many applications the parameter has direct scientific meaning, independent of the distribution it implies. Examples: a drug's true efficacy score ($\theta > 2$?), an election proportion ($\theta = 1/2$?), a regression coefficient ($\theta \neq 0$?). Because these are precise yes/no questions, they can often be answered more accurately than a full estimate of $\theta$ with confidence bounds — you are injecting more structure, so the data needs to do less work.

§5 Examples of Statistical Models

1. Bernoulli Trials

$\bigl(\{0,1\},\,(\text{Ber}(p))_{p\in(0,1)}\bigr)$

$\Theta = (0,1)$

Example: Does a couple kiss leaning right? $X_i = 1$ if yes.

2. Exponential

$\bigl(\mathbb{R}^*_+,\,(\text{Exp}(\lambda))_{\lambda>0}\bigr)$

$\Theta = (0,+\infty)$

Example: Waiting time between subway arrivals.

3. Poisson

$\bigl(\mathbb{N},\,(\text{Poiss}(\lambda))_{\lambda>0}\bigr)$

$\Theta = (0,+\infty)$

Example: Number of siblings; counts of rare events.

4. Gaussian (Full)

$\bigl(\mathbb{R},\,(\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2))_{(\mu,\sigma^2)\in\mathbb{R}\times\mathbb{R}^*_+}\bigr)$

$\Theta = \mathbb{R} \times (0,+\infty)$

Two-dimensional parameter: $\theta = (\mu, \sigma^2)$.

5. Gaussian (Known Variance)

$\bigl(\mathbb{R},\,(\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma_0^2))_{\mu\in\mathbb{R}}\bigr)$

$\Theta = \mathbb{R}$ (only $\mu$ unknown)

Example: Lab instrument with manufacturer-specified $\sigma_0^2$ on the label.

6. Uniform

$\bigl([0,1],\,(\text{Uniform}[0,\theta])_{\theta>0}\bigr)$

$\Theta = (0,+\infty)$

Note: The sample space $[0,1]$ must not depend on the unknown $\theta$.

Poisson PMF (Reference)

If $X \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$, then for $x = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$:

Poisson PMF
$$P(X = x) = \frac{\lambda^x e^{-\lambda}}{x!}$$

This PMF is strictly positive for all $x \geq 0$ and all $\lambda > 0$, which is precisely why the Poisson model extrapolates sensibly to unobserved count values.

Censored Data: A Special Case

A common real-world complication is censored (truncated) data. Suppose the true variable $Y \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$ but we can only observe whether $Y > 5$ (e.g., whether a subway delay exceeds 5 minutes). Then:

Censored Observation
$$X = \mathbf{1}[Y > 5] \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p), \quad p = P(Y > 5) = \int_5^\infty \lambda e^{-\lambda y}\, dy = e^{-5\lambda}$$

The model should be reparametrized by $\lambda > 0$ (the scientifically meaningful parameter), not $p$ directly, even though $X$ is Bernoulli.

§6 Model Identifiability

Once we estimate the distribution of $X$ exactly, can we recover $\theta$ uniquely? This is the question of identifiability.

Definition — Identified Parameter

The parameter $\theta$ is called identified if and only if the map $\theta \in \Theta \mapsto \mathbb{P}_\theta$ is injective:

$$\theta = \theta' \implies \mathbb{P}_\theta = \mathbb{P}_{\theta'}$$

Equivalently (contrapositive): $\mathbb{P}_\theta = \mathbb{P}_{\theta'} \implies \theta = \theta'$.

If the parameter is not identifiable, it cannot be recovered even from infinite data.

Counterexample: Non-Identifiable Parameterization

Suppose we observe $X_i = \mathbf{1}_{Y_i \geq 0}$, where $Y_1, \ldots, Y_n \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ for unknown $\mu \in \mathbb{R}$ and $\sigma^2 > 0$, but the $Y_i$ are unobserved. Then:

Bernoulli Parameter as a Function of $(\mu, \sigma)$
$$p = P(Y > 0) = P\!\left(\frac{Y - \mu}{\sigma} > -\frac{\mu}{\sigma}\right) = 1 - \Phi\!\left(-\frac{\mu}{\sigma}\right) = \Phi\!\left(\frac{\mu}{\sigma}\right)$$

Here $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF. The key observation: $p$ depends on $(\mu, \sigma)$ only through the ratio $\mu/\sigma$. Scaling both by the same constant — $(2\mu, 2\sigma)$, $(12\mu, 12\sigma)$, etc. — leaves $p$ unchanged. Thus $\mu$ and $\sigma$ individually are not identifiable.

Resolution

Both $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$ individually are not identified, but the ratio $\theta = \mu/\sigma$ is identified. The correct reparametrized model uses only $\rho = \mu/\sigma \in \mathbb{R}$ as the parameter: $\big(\{0,1\},\ \{\text{Bernoulli}(\Phi(\rho))\}_{\rho \in \mathbb{R}}\big)$, which is identified. The ratio $\mu/\sigma$ is interpretable as a normalized mean — how large the mean is relative to the standard deviation.

All four standard parametric families in §5 (Bernoulli, Exponential, Poisson, Gaussian) have identified parameters.

§7 Estimators and Their Properties

Definition — Estimator (Statistic)

An estimator $\hat{\theta}_n$ is any measurable function of the data $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ that does not depend on the unknown parameter $\theta$:

$$\hat{\theta}_n = \hat{\theta}(X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n)$$

An estimator is also called a statistic. A batting average, a sample mean, and a sample maximum are all statistics.

Practical measurability check: given data, can you compute the function? If yes, it is measurable. Problems may arise when functions are defined as infima/suprema over large function spaces that may not be attained.

Properties of Estimators

PropertyFormal StatementInterpretation
Unbiasedness $\mathbb{E}[\hat{\theta}_n] = \theta$ for all $\theta$ On average over many repetitions, the estimator hits the truth.
Consistency (weak) $\hat{\theta}_n \xrightarrow{\mathbb{P}} \theta$ as $n \to \infty$ (w.r.t. $\mathbb{P}_\theta$) With more data, the estimator converges in probability to $\theta$.
Consistency (strong) $\hat{\theta}_n \xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}} \theta$ as $n \to \infty$ (w.r.t. $\mathbb{P}_\theta$) Almost surely converges; $\bar{X}_n$ is strongly consistent by the strong LLN.
Low Quadratic Risk $\mathbb{E}[|\hat{\theta}_n - \theta|^2] \to 0$ Convergence in $L^2$; implies weak consistency.
Unbiasedness Alone is Not Enough

Both $\bar{X}_n$ and $X_1$ are unbiased estimators of the Bernoulli parameter $\theta$. Yet $X_1$ ignores all but the first observation — it cannot improve as $n$ grows. Bias is necessary but far from sufficient as a performance criterion.

Bias

Definition — Bias
$$\text{Bias}(\hat{\theta}_n) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\hat{\theta}_n\right] - \theta$$

The estimator is unbiased if $\mathbb{E}[\hat{\theta}_n] = \theta$, i.e., $\text{Bias}(\hat{\theta}_n) = 0$.

Quadratic (L²) Risk

Definition — Quadratic Risk
$$R(\hat{\theta}_n) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[|\hat{\theta}_n - \theta|^2\right]$$

The quadratic risk measures the expected squared deviation from the true parameter. It is the primary scalar summary of estimator performance. When $\Theta \subseteq \mathbb{R}$, this decomposes as:

$$\text{Quadratic risk} = \text{bias}^2 + \text{variance}$$

Example: Bernoulli Sample Mean

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \text{Bernoulli}(\theta)$, and let $\hat{\theta}_n = \bar{X}_n$.

Bias
$$\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}_n] = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[X_i] = \frac{1}{n} \cdot n\theta = \theta \implies \text{Bias} = 0$$
Variance (using independence)
$$\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n) = \frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(X_i) = \frac{n \cdot \theta(1-\theta)}{n^2} = \frac{\theta(1-\theta)}{n}$$
Quadratic Risk
$$R(\bar{X}_n) = 0^2 + \frac{\theta(1-\theta)}{n} = \frac{\theta(1-\theta)}{n}$$

As $n \to \infty$, $R \to 0$, confirming consistency. The risk is maximized at $\theta = \tfrac{1}{2}$ with value $\tfrac{1}{4n}$.

§8 The Bias–Variance Decomposition

The quadratic risk decomposes cleanly into two interpretable components.

Bias–Variance Decomposition
$$\underbrace{\mathbb{E}\!\left[(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta)^2\right]}_{\text{Quadratic Risk}} = \underbrace{\left(\mathbb{E}[\hat{\theta}_n] - \theta\right)^2}_{\text{Bias}^2} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\!\left[\left(\hat{\theta}_n - \mathbb{E}[\hat{\theta}_n]\right)^2\right]}_{\text{Variance}}$$

Derivation Sketch

Add and subtract $\mathbb{E}[\hat{\theta}_n]$ inside the square:

Expanding the Squared Error
$$(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta)^2 = \Bigl[(\hat{\theta}_n - \mathbb{E}[\hat{\theta}_n]) + (\mathbb{E}[\hat{\theta}_n] - \theta)\Bigr]^2$$ $$= \underbrace{(\hat{\theta}_n - \mathbb{E}[\hat{\theta}_n])^2}_{\text{variance term}} + 2\underbrace{(\hat{\theta}_n - \mathbb{E}[\hat{\theta}_n])}_{\text{zero-mean r.v.}} \cdot \underbrace{(\mathbb{E}[\hat{\theta}_n] - \theta)}_{\text{constant}} + (\mathbb{E}[\hat{\theta}_n] - \theta)^2$$

Taking expectations: the cross-term vanishes because $\mathbb{E}[\hat{\theta}_n - \mathbb{E}[\hat{\theta}_n]] = 0$, leaving exactly the bias–variance decomposition above.

Comparing Three Estimators of $\theta$ (Bernoulli)

EstimatorBiasVarianceQuadratic Risk
$\bar{X}_n$ $0$ $\theta(1-\theta)/n$ $\theta(1-\theta)/n$
$X_1$ only $0$ $\theta(1-\theta)$ $\theta(1-\theta)$
$0.5$ (constant) $0.5 - \theta$ $0$ $(0.5 - \theta)^2$
Key Insight — Bias-Variance Trade-off

The constant estimator $\hat{\theta} = 0.5$ has zero variance (it never fluctuates) but non-zero bias (unless $\theta = 0.5$ exactly). Near $\theta = 0.5$, its risk $(0.5 - \theta)^2$ can be lower than $\bar{X}_n$'s risk $\theta(1-\theta)/n$ for small $n$. As $n$ grows, $\bar{X}_n$ dominates everywhere since its risk shrinks as $1/n$.

Consequence: Quadratic Risk $\to 0$ Implies Consistency

If $R(\hat{\theta}_n) = \mathbb{E}[(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta)^2] \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$, then $\hat{\theta}_n \xrightarrow{L^2} \theta$, which implies $\hat{\theta}_n \xrightarrow{P} \theta$ (weak consistency). Moreover, since both bias² and variance are non-negative and must each tend to zero, this simultaneously guarantees:

  • Vanishing bias: the estimator targets the right value in the limit.
  • Vanishing variance: fluctuations around the truth shrink to zero.

§9 Confidence Intervals (Review)

Definition — Confidence Interval of Level $1-\alpha$

Let $\alpha \in (0,1)$. A confidence interval (CI) of level $1-\alpha$ for $\theta$ is any random interval $\mathcal{I}$ (depending on $X_1, \ldots, X_n$) whose boundaries do not depend on $\theta$, and such that:

$$\mathbb{P}_\theta\!\left[\mathcal{I} \ni \theta\right] \geq 1 - \alpha, \quad \forall\, \theta \in \Theta$$

The notation $\mathcal{I} \ni \theta$ emphasizes that the randomness lies in the interval $\mathcal{I}$, not in $\theta$.

Definition — Asymptotic Confidence Interval

A CI of asymptotic level $1-\alpha$ for $\theta$ is any random interval $\mathcal{I}$ whose boundaries do not depend on $\theta$, and such that:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \mathbb{P}_\theta\!\left[\mathcal{I} \ni \theta\right] \geq 1 - \alpha, \quad \forall\, \theta \in \Theta$$

Asymptotic CIs arise when using the Central Limit Theorem. They may not achieve the nominal coverage level for finite $n$.

Common Misconception

Once you observe data and compute a specific interval, e.g., $[0.42,\ 0.65]$, the statement "$P(\theta \in [0.42, 0.65]) = 0.95$" is incorrect. The realized interval is fixed; $\theta$ is also fixed. The probability is either 0 or 1. The $1-\alpha$ probability refers to the procedure: if you repeat the experiment many times, approximately $(1-\alpha) \times 100\%$ of the resulting intervals will contain $\theta$.

Construction: Bernoulli CI via CLT

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \text{Ber}(p)$ for unknown $p \in (0,1)$. By the Law of Large Numbers, $\bar{X}_n$ is a strongly consistent estimator of $p$.

Let $q_{\alpha/2}$ be the $(1 - \tfrac{\alpha}{2})$-quantile of $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. By the CLT:

Ideal (but infeasible) CI — depends on unknown $p$
$$\mathcal{I} = \left[\bar{X}_n - \frac{q_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{p(1-p)}}{\sqrt{n}},\; \bar{X}_n + \frac{q_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{p(1-p)}}{\sqrt{n}}\right]$$ $$\lim_{n\to\infty}\mathbb{P}_p\!\left[\mathcal{I} \ni p\right] = 1 - \alpha, \quad \forall\, p \in (0,1)$$
Problem

The interval $\mathcal{I}$ above depends on the unknown parameter $p$ — making it unusable in practice. We need to eliminate $p$ from the bounds.

Two solutions resolve this:

MethodIntervalComment
Conservative
(bound $p(1-p) \leq \tfrac{1}{4}$)		 $\bar{X}_n \pm \dfrac{q_{\alpha/2}}{2\sqrt{n}}$ Replaces $p(1-p)$ with its maximum value $\tfrac{1}{4}$. Always valid; interval is always wider.
Slutsky
(plug-in $\bar{X}_n$ for $p$)		 $\bar{X}_n \pm q_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{\dfrac{\bar{X}_n(1-\bar{X}_n)}{n}}$ By Slutsky's theorem, replacing $p$ with the consistent estimator $\bar{X}_n$ preserves the asymptotic level. Narrower intervals.

Here $q_{\alpha/2}$ is the $(1-\alpha/2)$-quantile of $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ — e.g., $q_{0.025} \approx 1.96$ for a 95% CI.

Choosing Between Methods

Use the conservative method when valid error bounds are mission-critical and you want guarantees beyond the asymptotic regime. Use Slutsky's method when extracting maximum precision from your data matters and asymptotic validity is acceptable. For finite-sample guarantees with no CLT approximation error, use Hoeffding's inequality, at the cost of substantially wider intervals.
