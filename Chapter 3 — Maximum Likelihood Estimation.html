Section 01

Motivation & The Statistician's Goal

We observe i.i.d. data \(X_1, \ldots, X_n\) drawn from an unknown true distribution \(\mathbb{P}_{\theta^*}\). Our task is to produce an estimator \(\hat\theta = \hat\theta(X_1,\ldots,X_n)\) such that the estimated distribution \(\mathbb{P}_{\hat\theta}\) is close to the true distribution \(\mathbb{P}_{\theta^*}\).

✦ Key Intuition

"Close distributions" means: for every event \(A \subseteq E\), \(\left|\mathbb{P}_{\hat\theta}(A) - \mathbb{P}_{\theta^*}(A)\right|\) is small. The worst-case departure over all events defines the total variation distance.

Knowing \(\theta^*\) is a proxy for knowing \(\mathbb{P}_{\theta^*}\) itself. The identification condition ensures one-to-one correspondence between parameters and distributions.

Section 02

Total Variation Distance

Definition — Total Variation

For two probability measures \(\mathbb{P}_\theta\) and \(\mathbb{P}_{\theta'}\) on sample space \(E\):

$$\mathrm{TV}(\mathbb{P}_\theta,\mathbb{P}_{\theta'}) = \max_{A \subseteq E}\left|\mathbb{P}_\theta(A) - \mathbb{P}_{\theta'}(A)\right|$$

If \(\mathrm{TV}(\mathbb{P}_{\hat\theta}, \mathbb{P}_{\theta^*}) \le 0.01\), then for every event \(A\): \(\mathbb{P}_{\hat\theta}(A) \in \bigl[\mathbb{P}_{\theta^*}(A)\pm 0.01\bigr]\).

Computable Forms

Computing a max over all subsets of \(E\) is intractable. Fortunately, the total variation simplifies when we have explicit PMFs or densities.

SettingComputable Formula
Discrete \(E\) (PMF \(p_\theta\)) \(\displaystyle\mathrm{TV}(\mathbb{P}_\theta,\mathbb{P}_{\theta'}) = \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_{x\in E}\left|p_\theta(x)-p_{\theta'}(x)\right|\)
Continuous \(E\) (density \(f_\theta\)) \(\displaystyle\mathrm{TV}(\mathbb{P}_\theta,\mathbb{P}_{\theta'}) = \tfrac{1}{2}\int_E\left|f_\theta(x)-f_{\theta'}(x)\right|\,dx\)

✦ Geometric Interpretation

For two density curves, \(2\cdot\mathrm{TV}\) equals the area between the two curves — the region where one density exceeds the other.

Properties (Metric Axioms)

  • Symmetry: \(\mathrm{TV}(\mathbb{P}_\theta,\mathbb{P}_{\theta'}) = \mathrm{TV}(\mathbb{P}_{\theta'},\mathbb{P}_\theta)\)
  • Non-negativity: \(\mathrm{TV} \ge 0\)
  • Definiteness: \(\mathrm{TV}(\mathbb{P}_\theta,\mathbb{P}_{\theta'}) = 0 \iff \mathbb{P}_\theta = \mathbb{P}_{\theta'}\)
  • Triangle inequality: \(\mathrm{TV}(\mathbb{P}_\theta,\mathbb{P}_{\theta'}) \le \mathrm{TV}(\mathbb{P}_\theta,\mathbb{P}_{\theta''}) + \mathrm{TV}(\mathbb{P}_{\theta''},\mathbb{P}_{\theta'})\)

These four properties confirm that TV is a genuine distance between probability distributions.

⚠ Practical Limitation

Although TV has a clear probabilistic meaning, it is difficult to estimate from data — there is no simple expression for TV as an expectation under \(\mathbb{P}_{\theta^*}\). This prevents us from replacing it by a sample average. We must use a surrogate.

Section 03

Kullback–Leibler Divergence

The KL divergence is a tractable surrogate for total variation that can be estimated from data because it is expressible as an expectation.

Definition — KL Divergence

$$\mathrm{KL}(\mathbb{P}_\theta, \mathbb{P}_{\theta'}) = \begin{cases} \displaystyle\sum_{x\in E} p_\theta(x)\log\!\frac{p_\theta(x)}{p_{\theta'}(x)} & \text{(discrete)} \\[12pt] \displaystyle\int_E f_\theta(x)\log\!\frac{f_\theta(x)}{f_{\theta'}(x)}\,dx & \text{(continuous)} \end{cases}$$

Crucially, the order of arguments matters: \(\mathrm{KL}(\mathbb{P}_\theta,\mathbb{P}_{\theta'}) \ne \mathrm{KL}(\mathbb{P}_{\theta'},\mathbb{P}_\theta)\) in general.

Properties

PropertyTVKL
Symmetric✓ Yes✗ No (in general)
Non-negative✓ Yes✓ Yes (by Jensen's inequality)
Definite (= 0 ⟺ equal)✓ Yes✓ Yes
Triangle inequality✓ Yes✗ No (in general)
Estimable from data✗ Hard✓ Yes (as an expectation)

Because KL is not symmetric and doesn't satisfy the triangle inequality, it is called a divergence rather than a distance. Nevertheless, definiteness is sufficient for our statistical purposes.

✦ Why Asymmetry Helps

Placing the true distribution \(\mathbb{P}_{\theta^*}\) in the first argument allows us to write the KL as an expectation under \(\mathbb{P}_{\theta^*}\) — the very distribution generating our data. This lets us replace the expectation by a sample average.

KL as an Expectation

Writing out the KL with \(\mathbb{P}_{\theta^*}\) as the first argument (continuous case):

①
\(\displaystyle\mathrm{KL}(\mathbb{P}_{\theta^*},\mathbb{P}_\theta) = \int f_{\theta^*}(x)\log\frac{f_{\theta^*}(x)}{f_\theta(x)}\,dx\)

Definition of KL.

②
\(\displaystyle = \mathbb{E}_{\theta^*}\!\left[\log f_{\theta^*}(X)\right] - \mathbb{E}_{\theta^*}\!\left[\log f_\theta(X)\right]\)

Split the logarithm of a ratio.

③
\(\displaystyle = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{\theta^*}[\log f_{\theta^*}(X)]}_{\text{constant w.r.t. } \theta} - \mathbb{E}_{\theta^*}\!\left[\log f_\theta(X)\right]\)

The first term is the negative entropy of \(\mathbb{P}_{\theta^*}\) — it does not depend on \(\theta\).

Minimizing \(\mathrm{KL}(\mathbb{P}_{\theta^*},\mathbb{P}_\theta)\) over \(\theta\) is therefore equivalent to maximizing \(\mathbb{E}_{\theta^*}[\log f_\theta(X)]\).

Section 04

From KL Estimation to MLE

Estimation Strategy

We cannot minimize \(\mathrm{KL}(\mathbb{P}_{\theta^*},\mathbb{P}_\theta)\) directly because \(\mathbb{P}_{\theta^*}\) is unknown. Instead:

  1. Write \(\mathrm{KL}(\mathbb{P}_{\theta^*},\mathbb{P}_\theta)\) as \(\text{const} - \mathbb{E}_{\theta^*}[\log f_\theta(X)]\).
  2. Estimate \(\mathbb{E}_{\theta^*}[\log f_\theta(X)]\) by the sample average \(\tfrac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \log f_\theta(X_i)\) — justified by the Law of Large Numbers.
  3. The unknown constant does not affect the minimizer over \(\theta\).
Estimated KL $$\widehat{\mathrm{KL}}(\mathbb{P}_{\theta^*},\mathbb{P}_\theta) = \text{"const"} - \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \log f_\theta(X_i)$$

Chain of Equivalences

\(\displaystyle\min_{\theta\in\Theta}\,\widehat{\mathrm{KL}}(\mathbb{P}_{\theta^*},\mathbb{P}_\theta)\)
⟺
\(\displaystyle\min_\theta\left(-\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n\log f_\theta(X_i)\right)\)

Drop the constant.

⟺
\(\displaystyle\max_\theta\sum_{i=1}^n\log f_\theta(X_i)\)

Flip sign; scaling by \(1/n\) does not change the argmax.

⟺
\(\displaystyle\max_\theta\log\prod_{i=1}^n f_\theta(X_i)\)

Sum of logs = log of product.

⟺
\(\displaystyle\max_\theta\prod_{i=1}^n f_\theta(X_i)\)

Log is strictly increasing; argmax is preserved.

✦ This Is Maximum Likelihood

The product \(\prod_{i=1}^n f_\theta(X_i)\) is the likelihood function — and maximizing it is precisely Maximum Likelihood Estimation. The entire derivation from KL divergence is what gives MLE its theoretical justification.

Section 05

Interlude: Maximizing Functions

MLE reduces to maximizing a function of \(\theta\). Understanding when this is tractable is important.

Concavity — the Key Property

Definition — Concave Function (1D)

A twice-differentiable function \(h:\Theta\subset\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}\) is concave if \(h''(\theta)\le 0\) for all \(\theta\in\Theta\), and strictly concave if \(h''(\theta) < 0\).

Convexity is the negation: \(h''(\theta)\ge 0\). Optimization textbooks use convexity (minimization); we need concavity (maximization).

Examples of Strictly Concave Functions

Function \(h(\theta)\)Domain \(\Theta\)\(h''(\theta)\)
\(-\theta^2\)\(\mathbb{R}\)\(-2 < 0\) ✓
\(\sqrt{\theta}\)\((0,\infty)\)\(-\tfrac{1}{4}\theta^{-3/2} < 0\) ✓
\(\log\theta\)\((0,\infty)\)\(-\theta^{-2} < 0\) ✓
\(\sin\theta\)\([0,\pi]\)\(-\sin\theta \le 0\) ✓
\(2\theta - 3\)\(\mathbb{R}\)\(0\) (concave but not strictly)

Why Concavity Makes Optimization Easy

  • A strictly concave function has at most one local maximum, which must be the global maximum.
  • To find it: set the derivative (or gradient) to zero and solve.
  • If \(h'(\theta^*) = 0\), then \(\theta^*\) is the unique global maximum.
  • Most classical statistical likelihoods are log-concave — this is precisely why MLE has closed-form solutions.

Multivariate Case

For \(h:\Theta\subset\mathbb{R}^d\to\mathbb{R}\), define:

  • Gradient: \(\nabla h(\theta) = \left(\tfrac{\partial h}{\partial\theta_1},\ldots,\tfrac{\partial h}{\partial\theta_d}\right)^\top\in\mathbb{R}^d\)
  • Hessian: \(\nabla^2 h(\theta)\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times d}\), where entry \((j,k)\) is \(\tfrac{\partial^2 h}{\partial\theta_j\partial\theta_k}\)

\(h\) is concave iff \(x^\top \nabla^2 h(\theta)\, x \le 0\) for all \(x\ne 0\), \(\theta\in\Theta\). To find the maximum: solve \(\nabla h(\theta) = \mathbf{0}\).

⚠ Boundary Warning

Setting the derivative to zero only finds an interior maximum. If the log-likelihood is monotone on the parameter space (no interior critical point), the maximum is attained at the boundary — e.g., the MLE may be \(\max_i X_i\). Always visualise the likelihood before differentiating.

Section 06

Likelihood and Log-Likelihood

Formal Definition

Definition — Likelihood

For an i.i.d. sample from a model with PMF \(p_\theta\) (discrete) or density \(f_\theta\) (continuous):

$$L_n(x_1,\ldots,x_n,\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^n p_\theta(x_i) \quad\text{or}\quad \prod_{i=1}^n f_\theta(x_i)$$

The likelihood is the joint distribution of the data, viewed as a function of the parameter \(\theta\).

Because many distributions involve exponentials, the log-likelihood is far more convenient to work with:

Log-Likelihood $$\ell_n(\theta) = \log L_n = \sum_{i=1}^n \log p_\theta(X_i) \quad\text{or}\quad \sum_{i=1}^n \log f_\theta(X_i)$$

Since \(\log\) is strictly increasing, \(\arg\max_\theta L_n = \arg\max_\theta \ell_n\). The log-likelihood is always preferred for computation.

Definition — Maximum Likelihood Estimator

$$\hat\theta_n^{\,\mathrm{MLE}} = \underset{\theta\in\Theta}{\arg\max}\; L_n(X_1,\ldots,X_n,\theta) = \underset{\theta\in\Theta}{\arg\max}\;\ell_n(\theta)$$

The MLE is the parameter value making the observed data most probable (or most plausible for continuous data).

Worked Examples of Likelihood Functions

Example — Bernoulli(\(p\))

Using the compact PMF \(p_p(x) = p^x(1-p)^{1-x}\):

$$L_n(p) = \prod_{i=1}^n p^{x_i}(1-p)^{1-x_i} = p^{\sum x_i}(1-p)^{n-\sum x_i}$$
Example — Poisson(\(\lambda\))

Using \(p_\lambda(x) = e^{-\lambda}\lambda^x/x!\):

$$L_n(\lambda) = e^{-n\lambda}\,\frac{\lambda^{\sum x_i}}{x_1!\cdots x_n!}$$

The denominator is constant in \(\lambda\) and can be ignored when maximising.

Example — Gaussian(\(\mu,\sigma^2\))
$$L_n(\mu,\sigma^2) = \frac{1}{(\sigma\sqrt{2\pi})^n}\exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i-\mu)^2\right)$$
Section 07

Computing the MLE — Worked Examples

Bernoulli Trials

Take the log-likelihood:

$$\ell(p) = \left(\sum_{i=1}^n x_i\right)\log p + \left(n - \sum_{i=1}^n x_i\right)\log(1-p)$$

Set derivative to zero:

$$\frac{d\ell}{dp} = \frac{\sum x_i}{p} - \frac{n - \sum x_i}{1-p} = 0$$

Solving:

$$\hat p_n^{\,\mathrm{MLE}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i = \bar X_n$$

Poisson Model

Log-likelihood (ignoring constants in \(\lambda\)):

$$\ell(\lambda) = \left(\sum_{i=1}^n x_i\right)\log\lambda - n\lambda$$

Set derivative to zero:

$$\frac{d\ell}{d\lambda} = \frac{\sum x_i}{\lambda} - n = 0$$
$$\hat\lambda_n^{\,\mathrm{MLE}} = \bar X_n$$

Gaussian Model

Taking the gradient of the Gaussian log-likelihood and setting it to zero yields:

$$\hat\mu_n^{\,\mathrm{MLE}} = \bar X_n, \qquad \hat\sigma^2_n{}^{\!\mathrm{MLE}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i - \bar X_n)^2 = \hat S_n$$

✦ A Satisfying Pattern

For all three standard models, the MLE turns out to be the sample mean (or a function of it). The sophisticated machinery of KL divergence and optimization confirms our natural intuition about averaging.

Section 08

Fisher Information

The Fisher information quantifies the curvature of the log-likelihood at its maximum, controlling how sensitive the MLE is to fluctuations in the data.

Definition — Fisher Information (1D)

Let \(\ell(\theta) = \log f_\theta(X)\) be the log-likelihood for a single observation. The Fisher information is:

$$I(\theta) = \mathrm{Var}_\theta[\ell'(\theta)] = -\mathbb{E}_\theta[\ell''(\theta)]$$

where expectations are taken under \(\mathbb{P}_\theta\).

Definition — Fisher Information Matrix (multivariate)

When \(\theta\in\mathbb{R}^d\):

$$I(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\nabla\ell(\theta)\nabla\ell(\theta)^\top\right] - \mathbb{E}[\nabla\ell(\theta)]\,\mathbb{E}[\nabla\ell(\theta)]^\top = -\mathbb{E}[\nabla^2\ell(\theta)]$$

Three Equivalent Formulas (continuous case)

#FormulaWhen to Use
1\(\mathrm{Var}_\theta[\ell'(\theta)]\)Often simplest
2\(-\mathbb{E}_\theta[\ell''(\theta)]\)Good when second derivative is clean
3\(\displaystyle\int_E \frac{\left(\partial_\theta f_\theta(x)\right)^2}{f_\theta(x)}\,dx\)Theoretical derivations

Proof that \(\mathrm{Var}[\ell'] = -\mathbb{E}[\ell'']\)

Key tools: (i) \(\int f_\theta(x)\,dx = 1\), so \(\frac{d}{d\theta}\int f_\theta(x)\,dx = 0\), which under regularity gives \(\int \partial_\theta f_\theta\,dx = 0\). Call this ★. Applying again: \(\int \partial^2_\theta f_\theta\,dx = 0\).

First, compute \(-\mathbb{E}[\ell'']\). Since \(\ell'' = \frac{f_\theta'' f_\theta - (f_\theta')^2}{f_\theta^2}\), multiplying through and integrating, the \(\int \partial^2_\theta f_\theta\,dx\) term vanishes by ★. We are left with \(\int\frac{(f'_\theta)^2}{f_\theta}dx\).

Second, compute \(\mathrm{Var}[\ell'] = \mathbb{E}[(\ell')^2] - (\mathbb{E}[\ell'])^2\). The second term equals \(\left(\int \partial_\theta f_\theta\,dx\right)^2 = 0\) by ★. The first term equals \(\int\frac{(f'_\theta)^2}{f_\theta}dx\).

Both expressions equal \(\int\frac{(\partial_\theta f_\theta)^2}{f_\theta}\,dx\), completing the proof.

Curvature Interpretation

Think of the log-likelihood \(\ell(\theta)\) as a hill centered at \(\theta^*\). The Fisher information measures how sharply peaked that hill is:

  • High \(I(\theta^*)\): the log-likelihood has a sharp, narrow peak → the MLE is robust and precise.
  • Low \(I(\theta^*)\): the log-likelihood is nearly flat → the MLE is sensitive to data fluctuations and has high variance.
Section 09

Asymptotic Properties of the MLE

Regularity Conditions

  1. The model is identifiable: \(\theta \mapsto \mathbb{P}_\theta\) is injective.
  2. The support of \(\mathbb{P}_\theta\) does not depend on \(\theta\).
  3. The true parameter \(\theta^*\) is in the interior of \(\Theta\).
  4. \(I(\theta)\) is invertible in a neighbourhood of \(\theta^*\).
  5. Technical smoothness conditions (permuting integrals and derivatives).

Theorem — Asymptotic Properties of the MLE

Under the above conditions:

(i) Consistency:

$$\hat\theta_n^{\,\mathrm{MLE}} \xrightarrow{\mathbb{P}} \theta^* \quad\text{as } n\to\infty$$

(ii) Asymptotic Normality:

$$\sqrt{n}\!\left(\hat\theta_n^{\,\mathrm{MLE}} - \theta^*\right) \xrightarrow{(d)} \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\, I(\theta^*)^{-1}\right) \quad\text{w.r.t. } \mathbb{P}_{\theta^*}$$

✦ What This Tells Us

Even when the MLE has no closed form, it behaves like a sample mean asymptotically. The asymptotic variance \(I(\theta^*)^{-1}\) is determined entirely by the Fisher information — no explicit computation of \(\mathrm{Var}[\hat\theta]\) needed.

Asymptotic Quadratic Risk

By the asymptotic normality result, the bias vanishes and the variance scales as \(1/n\):

$$\mathbb{E}_{\theta^*}\!\left[(\hat\theta_n^{\,\mathrm{MLE}} - \theta^*)^2\right] \approx \frac{1}{n\cdot I(\theta^*)}$$

A larger Fisher information means a smaller quadratic risk and a more efficient estimator.

Section 10

Method of Moments

Motivation via the Weierstrass Approximation Theorem

Any continuous bounded function on \([a,b]\) can be uniformly approximated by polynomials. In statistics, testing whether two distributions agree is equivalent to testing their integrals against all bounded continuous functions — and by Weierstrass, polynomials suffice. This reduces to matching moments.

Definition — Population and Empirical Moments

The \(k\)-th population moment is \(m_k(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_\theta[X^k]\).

The \(k\)-th empirical moment is \(\hat m_k = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^k\).

By the Law of Large Numbers, \(\hat m_k \xrightarrow{\mathbb{P}} m_k(\theta^*)\).

The Vandermonde Invertibility Result

For a discrete distribution with \(r\) possible values, the PMF is fully described by \(r-1\) free parameters. The first \(r-1\) moments are sufficient to recover the PMF, since the moment-to-PMF mapping is a linear system with a Vandermonde coefficient matrix:

$$\det(V) = \prod_{1\le j < k \le r}(x_k - x_j) \ne 0$$

Since the values \(x_1,\ldots,x_r\) are distinct, the determinant is nonzero and the matrix is invertible. Therefore moments and the PMF carry exactly the same information.

Method of Moments Estimator

Suppose \(\psi:\Theta\subset\mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^d\) maps parameters to the first \(d\) moments, and \(\psi\) is invertible. Then:

MoM Estimator $$\hat\theta_n^{\,\mathrm{MoM}} = \psi^{-1}(\hat m_1, \ldots, \hat m_d)$$

The rule of thumb: if \(\theta\in\mathbb{R}^d\), match \(d\) moments.

Asymptotic Properties

By the multivariate CLT and the delta method:

  1. Consistency: \(\hat\theta_n^{\,\mathrm{MoM}} \xrightarrow{\mathbb{P}} \theta^*\) (from LLN + continuous mapping theorem).
  2. Asymptotic normality: \(\sqrt{n}(\hat\theta_n^{\,\mathrm{MoM}} - \theta^*) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,\Sigma)\) where: $$\Sigma = \nabla\psi^{-1}(M(\theta^*))\cdot\Sigma_\theta\cdot\bigl[\nabla\psi^{-1}(M(\theta^*))\bigr]^\top$$ and \(\Sigma_\theta\) is the covariance matrix of the vector \((X, X^2, \ldots, X^d)\).

The gradient \(\nabla\psi^{-1}\) plays the same role as \(I^{-1}\) for MLE: a steep \(\psi^{-1}\) amplifies estimation error.

MLE vs. Method of Moments

CriterionMLEMethod of Moments
Statistical efficiencyHigher (asymptotically optimal)Often lower
Computational tractabilityRequires concave likelihoodAlways tractable (solve equations)
Use with non-concave likelihoodsOnly local heuristicsGood initialiser for local algorithms
Asymptotic variance\(I(\theta^*)^{-1}/n\)Depends on \(\nabla\psi^{-1}\) and \(\Sigma_\theta\)

✦ Practical Strategy

When the likelihood is non-concave, use the Method of Moments to obtain a good starting point, then run a local optimisation algorithm (gradient ascent, Newton–Raphson) from that point. MoM lands you in the right "basin of attraction"; MLE then refines the estimate.
